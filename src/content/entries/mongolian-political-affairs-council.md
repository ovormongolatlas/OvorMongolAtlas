The Mongolian Political Affairs Council (Монголын засгийн явдлын зөвлөл) grew directly out of the 1933 Inner Mongolian autonomy movement. After two rounds of talks at Bat-khaalag (Beile-yin sume) — a first meeting on July 26, 1933 and a larger second one on October 9, 1933 — and months of negotiation with envoys sent from Nanjing (Huang Shaohong and Zhao Piyan), a compromise body was inaugurated at Bat-khaalag on April 23, 1934 (Jagchid; Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). It governed under Nationalist Chinese sanction rather than as a fully independent state. "Bat-khaalag" is the Mongolian-source name for the same site the Chinese-derived literature and contemporary Western press call Bailingmiao or Pailingmiao — "Beile-yin sume," the Mongolian gloss given above, and "Bailingmiao" (百灵庙, "Prince's temple") are cognate names for one place, and Jagchid's and Zorigt's April 23, 1934 inauguration date matches the same event the *New York Times* reports below as occurring at "Pailingmiao"; this atlas treats the two names as denoting the same site throughout, per its name-mapping table.

Prince Yondonwangchug held the nominal chairmanship, but [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub) held only the formal title of secretariat chief; both Jagchid and Zorigt agree that, with the other senior princes elderly and largely inactive, real authority rested with Demchugdongrub alone (Jagchid; Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). Zorigt's account gives its jurisdiction as covering ten Shilingol banners, six Ulanchab banners, twelve Chahar banners, and seven Yekejuu banners (Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement").

Visiting Pailingmiao shortly after the council's inauguration, the *New York Times* correspondent Hallett Abend reported that its first major project was "the building up of a Central Mongolian army, something unknown in recent Mongolian history," with tribesmen arriving on horseback from across the region to train under "smartly uniformed Young Mongol graduates of Chinese and Japanese military academies" (Abend). See the [Inner Mongolian Army](/entry/inner-mongolian-army) entry for how this council-wide effort related to the army Demchugdongrub later built under his own authority.

The council's authority eroded as Japanese pressure on the region increased through 1935. In May 1936, Nanking ordered Demchugdongrub to dissolve the council and leave Pailingmiao; contemporary American press coverage tied the order to a recent Japanese court-martial and execution of four Hsingan-area Mongol leaders in Manchukuo, reported to have estranged Demchugdongrub from Japan as well, and speculated — as it turned out, inaccurately — that he might turn instead to Soviet Russia and Outer Mongolia ("Inner Mongolia Wavers"). The council was effectively superseded that same month by the Japanese-backed [Mongolian Military Government](/entry/mongolian-military-government).

## The assassination of Nimaodzar

One episode from this same erosion of Chahar Mongol political ground deserves recording on its own terms. Nimaodzar, a Chahar Mongol leader and member of the Chinese Kuomintang's Central Executive Committee whose views had grown increasingly anti-Japanese, was assassinated near the Houtou River on January 23, 1936, while traveling from Zhangbei back to Kalgan (Jagchid). (The year is fixed by Jagchid's own text: he places the killing in the days immediately before the Mongolian Army's February 12, 1936 founding ceremony, and dates an adjacent event to "the new lunar bingzi year," i.e. 1936 — this atlas records that date rather than any other that might be inferred from the episode's placement in a summary account.)

Jagchid's account, pieced together partly from his own knowledge and partly from later conversations with survivors of the ambush (Wang Ziyang, Li Jie'an, and Mergen), holds that the killing was engineered by the Japanese officer Morishima Kadofusa, with the assassin probably one Ogi; the killers wore blue uniforms to make the act look like the work of the Kuomintang's own "Blue Shirt Brigade" (Lanyishe), so as to inflame Mongol antagonism toward the Nationalist government (Jagchid, citing Lu Minghui for one detail). Japanese newspapers, including the *Tokyo Nichinichi* and the Good Neighbor Association's own monthly, then reported the resulting false version: that the Blue Shirts had eliminated Nimaodzar for his hostility to Nanjing (Jagchid). This atlas presents the engineered-assassination account as Jagchid's own reconstruction from multiple informants after the fact, not as an eyewitness narration of the killing itself; he was, by his own account, unusually close to it regardless, since Nimaodzar had personally issued him and Wu Heling an escape passport only shortly before.

Jagchid draws a direct consequence from the killing: it intimidated Jodbajab into silence, and effective power over the Chahar League passed to the Japanese "advisors," while the episode also soured Mongol-Japanese relations generally (Jagchid).

Writing contemporaneously in 1935, the American Sinologist Owen Lattimore offered an outside analytical view of why the movement culminating in this council arose when it did: he argued it was, in substance, a direct consequence of Japan's 1932 establishment of Manchukuo next door, which forced Mongol leaders to seek some form of self-rule quickly, before Tokyo or Nanjing settled Inner Mongolia's status without them (Lattimore, "Wickedness"). This is a claim about timing and motive rather than about who controlled the movement — it sits alongside, rather than against, [Jagchid](/entry/sechin-jagchid)'s and Zorigt's framing of the movement as Mongol-led (see the [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub) entry for that framing in full).
