Saichungga — the spelling used here, following the source; scholarly variants include Sayičungγa, Na. Sayinčoγtu, and Sainchogtu — was a poet and essayist whom Christopher Atwood reads as one of the founders of modern Inner Mongolian literature (Atwood). This entry rests almost entirely on Atwood's 1999 study, a peer-reviewed article by a leading historian of Inner Mongolia; it is treated here as the best available scholarly account, weighted at this atlas's top tier alongside Jagchid, but as a single-source portrait that later work may correct or deepen. Atwood's article reproduces his own English translations of Saichungga's 1945 poems and of the essay "A Ghost"; those translations are in copyright, so this entry paraphrases and quotes only sparingly.

## Education and early career

Saichungga was born in 1914 in the No. 2 sumu of the Plain-Blue Banner of Chakhar (in the modern Xulun Hoh banner), Inner Mongolia (Atwood). He attended primary school in his banner from 1929 to 1933 and, from 1936, a newly organized Mongolian high school; in the spring of 1937 he won a full scholarship to Japan, and in 1941 he graduated from Tōyō University in Tokyo (Atwood). His Japanese schooling places him in the same generation of Japan-educated Inner Mongolians the atlas follows elsewhere — among them [Khafungga](/entry/khafungga) and [Sechin Jagchid](/entry/sechin-jagchid) — a cohort the period's competing powers all sought to cultivate.

On returning he founded what Atwood calls western Inner Mongolia's first formal girls' school, the "West Sönid Certified Home Economist Girls' School" — a detail that sets him in the banner region of the [West Sünit ruling house](/entry/west-sunit-ruling-house), [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub)'s own — and helped edit textbooks with the "Develop Mongolia" organization in [Kalgan](/entry/kalgan) (Zhangjiakou) (Atwood). The institutional tie that most directly connects him to this atlas came in 1945, when for four months he served as secretary for cultural affairs to Demchugdongrub in his capacity as president of the [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state) (Atwood) — making Saichungga, however briefly, a Mengjiang cultural official rather than merely a contemporary of the regime.

## Writings

Atwood catalogues a compact but varied body of published work:

- *The Heart's Companion* — poems, written in Tokyo in 1939 and published in 1941.
- *Sand Dune Motherland* — a 1940 travel diary whose thinly veiled criticism of identifiable people drew controversy, though eastern Mongols, Atwood notes, read it instead as an encomium to pastoral life.
- *Light of the Heart* — 1942.
- *Notes on Home Improvement* — October 1942, addressed to Mongolian women, on households, education, science, and hygiene, in the stated service of building "a new Mongolia" (Atwood).
- *The Rising Song of Our Mongolia* — essays, April 1944.

Much of his work appeared in *Manduqu Mongghol* ("Mongolia Rising"), the Autonomous State's principal journal of opinion and literature, published from Kalgan and edited by Gombojab Hangin; its planned second volume was stillborn, killed by the August 1945 Soviet-Mongolian invasion (Atwood). It is from the surviving printer's sheets of that unpublished volume that Atwood recovered and first published the 1945 works — an essay and eight poems — on which much of his analysis turns.

## Significance

Atwood's reading is what gives Saichungga his place here. He presents Saichungga as a founder of modern Inner Mongolian literature whose cultural nationalism fused two strands that might seem to pull against each other: an intense reformist commitment to modernity, science, and moral renewal, and a romantic reverence for primordial images of Mongol nomadic life (Atwood). Characteristically, Atwood observes, Saichungga announced the supersession of Buddhist ethics using Buddhist metaphors to do it — reaching for the very tradition he was displacing. His 1945 poems, in Atwood's account, gave literary voice to the Japanese-sponsored, youth-centered, ascetic and martial nationalist mobilization of late-war Inner Mongolia, a youth movement increasingly set against the region's older aristocratic and religious authorities.

A feature of his reception illustrates a problem this atlas is attentive to — the instability of text and attribution. Atwood notes that many of Saichungga's poems circulated orally as songs, their authorship blurring in transmission: of five of his poems gathered in one Taiwan émigré folk-song collection, only two were credited to him, one was labeled an East Mongolian folk song, and another a Chakhar melody (Atwood). His words outran his name — a reminder that a poem's diffusion and its documented authorship can come apart.

## After 1945

Saichungga's later path echoes an arc this atlas traces in other lives, and is worth recording for that reason. After the August 1945 Soviet-Mongolian invasion he endorsed Marshal Choibalsan's goal of unifying Inner Mongolia with the Mongolian People's Republic, was appointed Chakhar's Deputy Head for Mongolian Banner Affairs, and in November 1945 travelled to Ulaanbaatar to study at the Sühbaatar Party Cadres Institute (Atwood). He graduated in 1947, returned to Inner Mongolia, became in effect the Chinese Communist Party's poet laureate of Inner Mongolia, and edited the *Inner Mongolian People's Daily*; in 1962 he repudiated the introspective, Buddhist-inflected romanticism of his earlier work (Atwood). The shape of that trajectory — Japanese-era cultural official, then pan-Mongol unificationist, then study in the MPR, then absorption into the Communist cultural apparatus, and finally persecution — resonates with the course the atlas records for [Khafungga](/entry/khafungga) and, in its persecuted end, for [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub); it is a pattern these lives share under the same pressures, not a rule to be read off from any one of them.

Atwood reports that Saichungga died on 13 May 1973 in Shanghai, following persecution and exile, while cautioning that no definitive biography of him exists — so the circumstances of his death are best held as Atwood's account rather than settled fact.
