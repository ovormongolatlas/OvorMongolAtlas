Merse — also known by his Chinese name, Guo Daofu (rendered elsewhere in Jagchid's book as Guo Taofu) — was a Dahur Mongol intellectual from eastern Inner Mongolia's Hulunbuir region, and one of the most influential nationalist organizers of his generation among the eastern Mongols (Jagchid). The start and end years given here mark the span of his political activity documented in the sources used for this entry, not birth and death dates, which are not established by those sources.

## From student radical to Hulunbuir politics

Merse studied at the Beijing College of Law and Politics, where he developed radical, nationalist views. He allied with a group of eastern Inner Mongolian youth to found the Mongolian Youth Party, then joined the Inner Mongolian People's Revolutionary Party under Bai Yunti's leadership (Jagchid). After Bai Yunti was pushed out by leftist rivals and left Ulaanbaatar, Merse was sent back to the Hulunbuir League. Having suffered these political setbacks, he moderated his leftist stance and built a working relationship with Lingsheng, the powerful conservative leader of Hulunbuir — who came to treat Merse as an advisor and, notably, sent him to Nanjing as his personal representative to welcome the Panchen Lama (Jagchid).

It was through the Panchen Lama's circle that Merse and [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub) became acquainted; Jagchid describes both men as sharing "the zeal and determination to struggle for Mongolian survival," and both sought the cooperation of the Mongol nobles gathered around the Panchen Lama for that cause (Jagchid).

## The Hulunbuir independence movement and the Normal Academy

In the winter of 1929, taking advantage of a Soviet military incursion into northern Manchuria, Merse and a former comrade, Fumingtai, launched a Hulunbuir independence movement centered on Hailar. When Soviet troops withdrew the following spring, Fumingtai left for the Mongolian People's Republic, while Merse accepted an offer from the warlord Zhang Xueliang to found the Normal Academy of the Northeast Mongolian Banners at Mukden (Jagchid). There he trained a generation of young Mongol nationalist intellectuals, seeding both nationalist and socialist ideas among his students; several went on to shape Inner Mongolia's later politics, education, and economy, including [Khafungga](/entry/khafungga), who Jagchid separately describes as "a former student of Merse" at this same school (Jagchid).

## Disintegration and disappearance

The Japanese seizure of Mukden during the Manchurian Incident of September 1931 deprived Merse of his institutional base: the Normal Academy, after only about two years of operation, disintegrated. Merse, deeply antagonized by Japan because of his earlier ties to the Comintern and the Inner Mongolian People's Revolutionary Party, returned to his home region of Hulunbuir. When Japanese forces subsequently invaded that area, he went on to Ulaanbaatar and, in Jagchid's account, "disappeared." Jagchid records a report that Merse was killed by Su Bingwen, a commander of Chinese remnant forces, but states explicitly that "there is no evidence to support this" — leaving his ultimate fate an open question rather than a settled fact (Jagchid).
