This entry rests substantially on a single source: a 2022 memorial slideshow by Sanj Altan, produced for the Mongol-American Cultural Association on the 33rd anniversary of Hangin's death. It is a community-memorial source — affectionate and commemorative in register rather than peer-reviewed scholarship — and is corroborated below against Christopher Atwood's 1999 study where the two overlap; where a claim rests on the slideshow alone, that is noted, and one specific figure below (his mother's death date) is flagged rather than stated flat because it does not square with the rest of the timeline as given.

## Family and early life

Hangin was born in 1921 into the Hangin family of the Plain White Banner (Taipus Left-Wing Herd, Chakhar), later reorganized as the Taipus Left Wing Banner (Sanj Altan). His father, Fullehengge (1897–1936), and grandfather, Samtanlhungrub — known by the honorific "Sa Amban" (1878–1932) — both figure in the slideshow's family record; his mother is given as Galindari (1899–2001) (Sanj Altan). That maternal date is flagged rather than asserted as settled: if accurate, it would make her about 102 at death and mean she outlived her son by twelve years, which is possible but notable enough that this atlas records it with that caveat rather than treating it as confirmed. Hangin married Sarangerel (b. 1923) and was one of four siblings (Sanj Altan). One of those siblings was the poet and essayist [Saichungga](/entry/saichungga) — a family tie this atlas can state because it links two figures already documented here, though it should be flagged rather than smoothed over: Saichungga's own entry, following Atwood, gives his banner of origin as the Plain-Blue Banner of Chakhar, not the Plain White Banner given here for the Hangin family. Both banners were part of the broader Chakhar grouping, so the discrepancy may reflect different administrative registrations, a half-sibling relationship, or an imprecision in one source or the other; this atlas does not attempt to resolve it. Hangin studied Chinese, Mongol, and Manchu from a young age (Sanj Altan).

## Mengjiang era

Hangin went to Japan in 1937 for preparatory schooling, and in 1938 — persuaded, per the slideshow, by [Wu Heling](/entry/wu-heling) — took up forestry studies at Hokkaido University (Sanj Altan). This Wu Heling connection is recorded here as the source states it; it would be a third documented tie between Hangin and Wu Heling, alongside Hangin's later work on Wu-era Mengjiang institutions, and is flagged rather than treated as independently corroborated, since only the slideshow attests it. He returned to Inner Mongolia in December 1941 and taught at the Mongolian Reconstruction school in [Kalgan](/entry/kalgan) and at the West Sönid girls' school — the same school in the [West Sünit ruling house](/entry/west-sunit-ruling-house)'s home region that Atwood, in the Saichungga entry, credits Saichungga with founding, meaning this atlas's sources place both brothers teaching at the same institution (Sanj Altan; cf. Atwood via the Saichungga entry). In 1944 he was appointed to what the slideshow calls the Office of the President's Secretariat under Demchugdongrub — this atlas's other entries on the period generally render Demchugdongrub's title as "chairman" rather than "president," a minor terminology difference recorded here rather than resolved (Sanj Altan). He remained through the 1945–46 collapse of Mengjiang before leaving for the United States in 1948 (Sanj Altan).

Hangin also edited *Manduqu Mongγol* ("Mongolia Rising"), the [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state)'s principal Mongol-language literary journal, published from Kalgan (Sanj Altan) — a claim independently corroborated by Atwood's study of Saichungga, whose work appeared in the same journal under Hangin's editorship (see the [Saichungga](/entry/saichungga) entry). This is one of the few points where the slideshow's Mengjiang-era detail can be checked against an independent scholarly source, and the two agree.

## In the United States

Hangin emigrated to the United States and became a research fellow on the Johns Hopkins Mongol Project from 1948 to 1953, earned an MA at Columbia, and completed a PhD at Indiana University in 1970 (Sanj Altan). In 1962 he founded the Mongolia Society, and he went on to become a major lexicographer of Mongolian, producing a 1986 *Modern Mongolian–English Dictionary* among other works (Sanj Altan). He was a public advocate for American diplomatic recognition of the Mongolian People's Republic, including a 1977 letter to the *New York Times* (Sanj Altan). In 1987, together with Tsorj Lama and Lopsang Khiyod, he co-founded the annual US Chinggis Khan Memorial Ceremony, an Inner Mongolian émigré commemoration that continues today under the Chinggis Khaan Memorial Foundation and the Mongol-American Cultural Association — see the [Chinggis Khan worship / imagery](/entry/chinggis-khan-worship-imagery) entry for that ceremony as a diaspora strand of the same symbol's political history (Sanj Altan). Hangin died in 1989.

## *Zangiy-a* ("The Knot")

In 1989 Hangin wrote a poem titled *Zangiy-a* ("The Knot"), which Sanj Altan's account describes as imagining Mongol identity as an invisible knot tightening across his life, from age ten to sixty, resolving in the realization that the knot is the single word "Mongol," bestowed on him by his parents. This atlas does not have the poem's text in hand and, in any case, this atlas's practice for in-copyright literary material is to paraphrase rather than reproduce it, with at most a short attributed quote where wording matters — that constraint, combined with not holding the text directly, is why only this description is given here rather than any quotation.
