"Mengjiang" is not the formal name of any single government; it is the name that came into common use — in Chinese, Japanese (as Mōkyō), and subsequently in English-language scholarship — for the Japanese-sponsored Mongol autonomous territory administered from Kalgan and Hohhot between the mid-1930s and 1945. Jagchid dates the name's emergence specifically to the formation of the Japanese-run "Joint Committee of Mongolian Territories" on November 22, 1937, which unified [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub)'s Mongol government with separate Japanese puppet regimes in Southern Chahar and Northern Shanxi (Jagchid). In looser usage, "Mengjiang" is sometimes applied back to the whole run of Demchugdongrub-led governments starting with the [Mongolian Military Government](/entry/mongolian-military-government) of 1936, since it was the first of these governments to be explicitly Japanese-sponsored — hence the startYear given here is approximate.

Across its existence the territory was governed by a succession of differently named administrations, each with its own entry in this atlas:

- [Mongolian Military Government](/entry/mongolian-military-government) (1936–1937)
- [Mongolian Allied League Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-league-autonomous-government) (1937–1939)
- [Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-autonomous-government) (1939–1941)
- [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state) (1941–1945), the final and internationally best-known iteration

Demchugdongrub led all four in some capacity, though not always as full chairman from the outset — see his entry and each government's own entry for the specifics of when he held nominal versus real authority. The territory dissolved with Japan's defeat in August 1945.
