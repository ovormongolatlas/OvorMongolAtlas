Kanai Shōji is named across many of this atlas's entries on the Mengjiang governments as the Japanese "Supreme Advisor" (最高顧問) whose office, more than any single Mongol official's, held real financial, police, and transport power behind [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub)'s formal chairmanship — this entry gathers that scattered material into one place rather than introducing new claims about him. The years given here bracket his documented tenure as Supreme Advisor specifically, not a birth-death span, which no source used in this atlas supplies.

## Supreme Advisor to the Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government

Kanai directed the September 1, 1939 founding ceremony of the [Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-autonomous-government) in the courtyard of the [United Committee of Mengchiang](/entry/united-committee-of-mengchiang), and was installed as that government's Supreme Advisor (Jagchid). Per Boyle, Demchugdongrub had resigned that April as Director-General of the United Committee of Mengchiang in dissatisfaction with Japanese policy, and Kanai took over that role as well (Boyle, p.133) — this atlas's master chronology and the United Committee of Mengchiang entry treat this as the same office described from two different sources.

Kanai used his position to shape Mongol politics directly rather than only administratively. He promoted the appointment of Prince Sungjingwangchug as Minister of Civilian Affairs, hoping to build up a rival who could eventually replace Demchugdongrub — a plan that, per Jagchid (who became Prince Sung's secretary), backfired when Sung proved loyal and Demchugdongrub came to rely on him as a trusted intermediary with the Japanese instead (Jagchid; see the Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government entry). Kanai also arranged [Wu Heling](/entry/wu-heling)'s effective exile to Japan under the pretext of deepening his knowledge of the country, removing a senior Mongol rival from Mengjiang politics; Jagchid, who accompanied Wu there from 1938 as his secretary, gives this episode in detail in the Wu Heling entry (Jagchid).

[Sechin Jagchid](/entry/sechin-jagchid) frames the broader relationship between the two men as an ongoing, losing political battle for Demchugdongrub: the concentration of Mengjiang's central institutions at [Kalgan](/entry/kalgan) from late 1937 onward "consolidated the authority of the Japanese supreme advisor Kanai Shoji at the prince's expense" (Jagchid; see the Kalgan entry).

## Decline and replacement

Kanai's dominance did not last through the whole Mengjiang period. Late in 1941, under the [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state), he was removed and succeeded by Ohashi Chuichi, who himself went on to clash with the Japanese military over the state's autonomy (Jagchid; see the Mongolian Autonomous State entry). A visit to Kalgan that same year by the Japanese army minister Tojo Hideki, who presented Demchugdongrub with 500 rifles for a personal bodyguard, is recorded elsewhere in this atlas as a signal that Kanai no longer dominated Mongol affairs even before his formal removal (Jagchid; see the Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government entry).

This atlas holds no source giving Kanai's own background, career before 1939, or fate after 1941 — this entry accordingly covers only his documented Mengjiang-era tenure, drawn entirely from material already cited elsewhere in this atlas plus the master chronology's cross-reference to Boyle.
