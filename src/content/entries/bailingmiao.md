This site carries three overlapping identities in this atlas's sources, and its names track them. Jagchid calls the monastery Beile-yin sume ("the temple of the beile"), colloquially "Beilin-sum," and notes that the Chinese name, Bailingmiao, is "a phonetic approximation of the Mongolian" (Jagchid). Elsewhere he pairs the names directly: "the well-known Beile-yin sume temple (Bailingmiao) was located at Bat-khaalag." This atlas's house spelling for the site is Bat-khaalag, used elsewhere in these entries; Bailingmiao (also romanized Pailingmiao) and Beile-yin sume name the same place. See this atlas's project notes for the full resolution of that naming question.

## A religious center

Bat-khaalag was, first, a monastery long held holy by Prince Yondonwangchug's banner. That standing mattered directly in 1936: when the site became a military target, Yondonwangchug specifically opposed turning "this famous religious center, long held holy by his banner, into a battlefield" (Jagchid). His later refusal to meet the victorious Chinese commander, described below, follows from this same religious attachment to the place.

## A political center: the Mongolian Political Affairs Council

Bat-khaalag was also, from 1933, the seat of the movement that became the [Mongolian Political Affairs Council](/entry/mongolian-political-affairs-council), formally inaugurated there on April 23, 1934. In Jagchid's account it functioned as "the center of the Mongolian autonomy movement," and was "well-known throughout China" (Jagchid) — a reputation that later made it a symbolically loaded military target, not merely a strategic one.

That political centrality did not last. After the death of Prince Sodnamrabdan ("Prince So" in Jagchid's telling), whose influence over [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub) had helped hold off Japanese pressure, the Nanjing government ordered the Mongolian Political Council disbanded and its officials to withdraw from Bat-khaalag (Bailingmiao), replacing it with a new Chahar Provincial Mongolian Political Council seated at [Jabsar](/entry/jabsar) (Jagchid). Demchugdongrub did not accept the withdrawal demand outright, and most of the council's main officials had in practice already relocated to Sunid and Jabsar; only a small military contingent, reorganized after an earlier rebellion of the Peace Preservation troops, remained at Bat-khaalag itself (Jagchid).

## The Battle of Bailingmiao, November 1936

As the separate Battle of Eastern Suiyuan bogged down that autumn, Demchugdongrub ordered Mugdenbuu's Seventh Division of the [Mongolian Army](/entry/inner-mongolian-army) to hold Bat-khaalag against occupation. Jagchid explains why the site drew Chinese attention specifically: it was, again, "the center of the Mongolian autonomy movement," so that occupying it would carry heavy symbolic weight and a psychological blow to the Mongols beyond its military value (Jagchid). Yan Xishan and Fu Zuoyi, planning the attack, exaggerated both the site's importance and Japanese strength in their reports to Chiang Kai-shek; Jagchid singles out one claim, that Japan was supplying "airplanes, tanks and poison gas" to Mongol forces, as "a complete fabrication of Yan's," made to inflate the sense of threat and win Chiang's authorization (Jagchid). Chiang ordered the attack in a telegram of November 16.

On the evening of November 23, 1936, a brigade under Sun Lanfeng of Fu Zuoyi's 35th Army marched from Wuchuan together with a Chinese Seventh Division under Wang Jingguo, while Shanxi troops under Zhao Chengshuo advanced from Guyang. (This attacking "Seventh Division" under Wang Jingguo is a distinct, Chinese, formation, not to be confused with the defending Mongolian Army's own Seventh Division under Mugdenbuu — the coincidence of numbering is Jagchid's own text, not an error introduced here.) Mugdenbuu's outnumbered Mongolian division was caught off guard and forced to retreat; in the resulting chaos the temple's monks escaped, and the Japanese advisors and Special Service Office personnel fled northeast alongside Mugdenbuu. By dawn, Fu Zuoyi's forces held Bat-khaalag (Jagchid).

What happened next is where this atlas's account has to hold two things side by side rather than settle on one. The Battle of Bailingmiao was, in plain historical terms, the first real Chinese victory against Japanese or Japanese-aligned forces since the Manchurian Incident of September 18, 1931, and it was treated as exactly that at the time: universities and colleges in Beiping sent delegations to the front to congratulate the troops, Fu Zuoyi became a national hero overnight, and the Chinese press celebrated the victory on its front pages (Jagchid). One newspaper, as Jagchid quotes it, reported that "300 Suiyuan troops were killed or wounded; on the enemy side, 300 were killed, 600 wounded and 600 captured."

Jagchid, writing decades later but drawing on his own repeated visits to the temple in the years after the battle and on conversations with the monks who had been there that night, disputes the specifics of that celebrated account without disputing that a real Chinese victory occurred. He states plainly that "reports such as these were all false" as to their detail. Contrary to what some believed, Fu's troops did not burn the temple after the monks fled, but they did destroy its images, scriptures, ritual instruments, doors, and windows, and looted and wrecked the lamas' dormitories (Jagchid). The real Japanese death toll is not known, but by Jagchid's account only about 100 Mongols were killed or wounded, far below the press's figures. This atlas records Jagchid's correction as his own participant-adjacent, monk-informed reconstruction, made after the fact, not as an eyewitness account of the battle itself, alongside the nationally celebrated version rather than in place of it; neither is presented here as the final word.

Prince Yondonwangchug, consistent with his opposition to the fighting in the first place, stayed at his residence some thirty-five kilometers away throughout Fu's occupation of the temple. His refusal to meet Fu Zuoyi was widely applauded by fellow Mongols (Jagchid).

Mugdenbuu's division, together with its Japanese advisors, retreated eastward to the Shira-Muren monastery in the Derben Keuked Banner, where a further engagement followed that December; that episode is not detailed in this entry (see this atlas's project notes, where it is logged as a candidate for future treatment). The defeat at Bat-khaalag itself was a serious blow to Demchugdongrub and to the wider autonomy movement, coming during the same Suiyuan campaign covered in the [Mongolian Military Government](/entry/mongolian-military-government) and [Inner Mongolian Army](/entry/inner-mongolian-army) entries.
