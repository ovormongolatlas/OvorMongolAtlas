This entry follows a single connective thread this atlas's master chronology identifies running underneath many separately-documented episodes elsewhere in this atlas: Chinese agricultural reclamation of Mongol banner pasture (放垦 / 開墾, *kaihuang*, "opening wasteland"). The thread's presence across so many periods and political regimes — Qing, Republican warlord, Manchukuo, Mengjiang, and finally the Chinese Communist Party itself — is the chronology's own framing, offered here as an organizing lens for episodes this atlas already documents individually, not as a single source's continuous narrative.

## Precursor: Jindandao, 1891

The earliest node in this thread predates this atlas's core period. In November–December 1891, a Han secret-society revolt across the Josotu and Juu Uda leagues massacred Mongols, with casualty estimates ranging so widely (roughly 150,000 to 500,000, depending on the source) that this atlas treats the figure as unsettled rather than picking a number. The revolt was suppressed by late December, but its consequence for Mongol banner authority was lasting: Beijing began routing Mongol-Han disputes and tax collection through Chinese provincial authorities rather than the banners themselves, weakening banner autonomy going forward. This atlas holds only reference-tier material on Jindandao itself — see the [Jindandao incident](/entry/jindandao-incident) stub entry — but records the master chronology's own framing of it as the thread's earliest precursor, a massacre whose underlying land and taxation tensions echo through everything that follows.

## The Gada Meiren uprising, 1929–31

The clearest instance of this grievance producing an armed uprising is Gada Meiren's. In 1929, the Fengtien warlord administration under Zhang Xueliang colluded with banner authorities to sell or reclaim roughly three-quarters of Darhan Banner's grassland (Khorchin Left Wing Middle Banner, Jirim League) to Han settlers. Gada Meiren, a banner militia officer (meiren), led resistance that grew into armed uprising, until he was surrounded and killed at the Shar Mörön river around April 4–5, 1931, by a Fengtien force this atlas's chronology names as led by [Li Shouxin](/entry/li-shouxin) — a detail now added to Li Shouxin's own entry, tagged SINGLE-SOURCE PERPETRATOR ACCOUNT and reference-tier, since it rests on Li's own late-life memoir and an unconfirmed encyclopedic date; see that entry and the [Gada Meiren](/entry/gada-meiren) stub entry for the full sourcing picture and its limits.

Mongol communal memory of the episode diverges from the documented facts in a specific, recorded way: a folk song and a 1979 narrative poem (composed roughly in the 1950s) both cast the warlord Zhang Zuolin as the villain — but Zhang Zuolin was assassinated in 1928, before the Darhan land sale that provoked the uprising; the actual warlord backer of the reclamation scheme was his son Zhang Xueliang's Fengtien regime. Some lower-tier sources also wrongly identify the settlers as Japanese. This atlas's master chronology records this as an explicit, unresolved conflict between folk memory and documented fact, and this entry preserves that same conflict rather than correcting the folklore or silently adopting it as history.

Manchukuo's founding in March 1932 briefly reversed the pressure: the new state adopted what this atlas's chronology calls a "Mongolian land-protection policy," aborting the Fengtien-era colonization projects and sparing Darhan Banner from further reclamation, at least for a time — a Japanese-administered exception to the pattern, not its end.

## The Hua Teh encroachment, 1934

The same grievance, on a smaller and more administrative scale, produced the renaming affair covered in full in the [Jabsar](/entry/jabsar) entry: Chahar provincial authorities created a new Chinese hsien, Hua Teh (化德), on Jabsar banner land in 1934, in violation of an agreement barring further Chinese administrative encroachment on Mongol territory. That entry covers the wordplay this act provoked from [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub) and the competing benign and pointed etymologies for the name, in full; this theme entry notes only that the underlying grievance — Chinese administrative and agricultural expansion onto banner land — is the same one running through the rest of this thread.

## Inversion: the CCP's 1947–52 reclamation drive

The thread's clearest turn comes after 1945, when the political valence of reclamation inverts. Per Pount's account of 1950s Inner Mongolian land reform (this atlas holds a fuller entry on this material, currently withheld from publication pending a maintainer scope decision on how far past 1945 this atlas should reach — see this atlas's project notes), a "reclamation fever" during the CCP's own 1947–52 land-reform period turned an estimated twelve million *mu* of pasture into farmland — the same underlying process (Chinese agricultural expansion onto Mongol pastoral land) that drove Jindandao, Gada Meiren, and the Hua Teh affair, now occurring under a nominally pro-Mongol Communist government rather than being imposed on Mongols by an openly rival Chinese or Japanese authority. What is new is who moved to stop it: in 1952, Ulanhu — an Inner Mongol who had risen to lead the Chinese Communist Party's own Inner Mongolian Autonomous Region — issued the policy "protect the pastures, forbid reclamation" (保护牧场，禁止开荒), halting the drive. Where every earlier episode in this thread pitted Mongol banner interests against an external Chinese or Japanese authority driving reclamation, this final episode has a Mongol figure inside the ruling party moving to stop it instead — the same grievance, its political shape inverted.
