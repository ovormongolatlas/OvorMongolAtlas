## Name and early life

Demchugdongrob — the spelling varies across sources; [Sechin Jagchid](/entry/sechin-jagchid)'s biography renders it "Demchugdongrob," while Mongolian-language accounts by researcher D. Zorigt use "Demchigdonrov" — was born in 1902, a thirty-first-generation descendant of Chinggis Khan through the Sunid line of the Golden Clan (Jagchid). Sources disagree on exactly where: Jagchid places his birth in the Sunid Right Banner itself, while Zorigt's article on the autonomy movement states he was born in the Shuluun Tsagaan Banner of the Chahar League, his mother's home banner. That account adds a specific reason for the discrepancy — his elderly father Namjilwangchug, alarmed that his own birth year and his newborn son's shared the "tiger" sign, reportedly kept mother and child away from him out of superstition, and never met his heir before dying in 1902 (Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). Jagchid adds a detail that helps explain why sources disagree on the exact date: by the traditional lunar calendar Demchugdongrub was born on the day, month, and year of the tiger — an auspicious "four-tiger" birth — but he was born in the morning without the hour being recorded, in keeping with a Mongol custom of not divulging a birth time lest it invite a curse, so that his birthday was never officially celebrated; Jagchid places the Western-calendar equivalent at the 12th or 17th of February 1902 (Jagchid). Both sources agree Namjilwangchug's death left the succession unresolved for years. During Demchugdongrub's minority the senior tusalagchi (assistant noble) Pandei held the banner's tamaga (official seal) and administered on his behalf; the Qing court did not confirm the succession until 1908, when Demchugdongrub was invested with the hereditary title of jasag toroyin junwang (ruling prince, second rank) of the [Sunid Right Banner](/entry/west-sunit-ruling-house) at around age six — the six-year delay caused, in Jagchid's account, by Beijing officials who stalled the paperwork until bribed (Jagchid; Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). Because Mongolian custom considered it impolite to use an elder or a person of rank's full personal name, he was addressed by the honorific short forms De noyan or De wangye, giving him the widely used English name "Prince De" (Jagchid).

Pandei continued to administer the banner until 1919, when Demchugdongrub reached his majority (eighteen nasun) and assumed personal rule; in 1924, at twenty-three, he was promoted to deputy head of the Shilingol League (Jagchid; Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). During his minority he had already been raised in rank on paper: to court Mongol support after the fall of the Qing, Yuan Shikai's Republican government confirmed and elevated many hereditary titles, and the young Demchugdongrub was promoted to first-ranking prince, Khoshoi Dugureng Chin Wang, a distinction warmly received in the conservative Sunid Right Banner (Jagchid).

In 1929, the Swedish explorer Sven Hedin passed through the region and recorded the household directly. Hedin never uses a personal name — he refers only to "Sunit Wang," the Wang (king) of Barun Sunit (West Sünit) and sovereign of the Sünid Mongols — but the title and banner match Demchugdongrub's own at the time, per Jagchid. Hedin locates the prince's headquarters at a palace called Wangin Ortu ("the King's Camp"), built in Chinese style with a private temple (Hedin). Notably, Hedin records that the Panchen (Tashi) Lama, on his 1929 tour of Inner Mongolia, stayed at Wangin Ortu itself for about a month that July and August, with roughly 10,000 pilgrims camped around it; a separate gathering that June at another monastery had drawn an estimated 30,000 Mongols in total, of whom about 2,000 came from Barun Sunit itself (Hedin). When Hedin's own party reached the palace that November, the prince was away — he had accompanied the Panchen Lama on the onward journey toward Mukden, so the two never met (Hedin). Hedin also records the banner's army at about a thousand men and the prince's household as possessing three motor cars (Hedin).

## Leading the Inner Mongolian autonomy movement

Through the late 1920s Demchugdongrub built an independent base of power inside his own banner: schools, a clinic, a small textile mill, even a workshop producing small arms and ammunition, backed by a personal guard force that grew past a thousand men by 1926 (Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). He also began cultivating outside patrons — meeting Zhang Xueliang in Mukden in 1929 to source weapons, then Chiang Kai-shek in Wuhan in 1932 to discuss forming a Mongol cavalry division — and spent the winter of 1932 in Beijing courting the city's Mongol student community, laying the groundwork for the movement's youth support (Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement").

By the early 1930s he had emerged as the figure willing to press for greater Mongol self-rule against both Chinese provincial encroachment and the threat of Japanese expansion. Jagchid, who was personally close to him, describes 1933 as Prince De's "golden age": working through personal networks with other Mongol nobles — most importantly Prince Sodnamrabdan of Ujumuchin and Prince Yondonwangchug, head of the Ulanchab League, whom he persuaded in secret talks at Bat-khaalag — he built enough elite support to publicly demand a "higher degree of autonomy" for Inner Mongolia from the Nanjing government (Jagchid). Zorigt's account dates the first formal self-rule meeting at Bat-khaalag to July 26, 1933, followed the next day by a telegram to Nanjing demanding autonomy; a larger second meeting opened on October 9, 1933, a date on which Jagchid's and Zorigt's accounts agree (Jagchid; Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). Nanjing sent envoys — Huang Shaohong and Zhao Piyan — to negotiate, and after months of dispute over whether the new body would be a full "autonomous government" or a lesser "local self-rule committee," a compromise [Mongolian Political Affairs Council](/entry/mongolian-political-affairs-council) was inaugurated at Bat-khaalag on April 23, 1934, with Yondonwangchug as nominal chairman. Demchugdongrub himself held only the formal title of secretariat chief, but with the other senior princes elderly and largely inactive, both sources agree real power rested with him alone (Jagchid; Zorigt, "Self-Rule Movement"). Jagchid frames the movement as genuinely Mongol-driven rather than a Japanese creation, quoting a purported memoir of Demchugdongrub in which a Japanese agent is said to have complained that the Mongols had launched their autonomy push before Japanese forces were ready to back it.

American press coverage from the time adds independent detail and one notable discrepancy. The *New York Times* reported the council's inauguration with 28 members drawn from Mongolian leagues as far away as Kokonor, funding from Nanjing of about $50,000 a month, and a first stated priority of building a unified Mongolian army — a goal that would not be realized in institutional form until the Mongolian Army General Headquarters ceremony almost two years later (Abend). The same report described Demchugdongrub not as secretariat chief but as vice chairman of the Shilingol delegation, and quoted him stating that Japanese agents had pressed the assembled princes in March 1934 to seek annexation to Manchukuo, and had been rebuffed (Abend). The differing title is recorded here rather than reconciled with Jagchid and Zorigt's account, since contemporary foreign press coverage of internal Mongol administrative titles was not always precise.

A Welsh journalist, Gareth Jones, independently corroborates both the West Sünit setting and Demchugdongrub's palace: in a letter dated July 14, 1935, Jones describes visiting him there and being a guest at a large Mongol feast honoring the mountain spirit (Jones). Jones gives a firsthand physical description — a large man in dark blue silk, a black skull-cap with a red button, and a long queue — though he guessed the prince's age at about 45 to 50; Demchugdongrub was in fact 33 in 1935, so this particular impression was inaccurate, a useful reminder that even a careful eyewitness's snap judgments can be wrong (Jones). More substantively, in an interview recorded in the same letter, Demchugdongrub told Jones he aimed to build an independent Mongol empire uniting the Mongols of Inner Mongolia with those of Soviet-aligned Outer Mongolia and those under Manchukuo — his pan-Mongol ambition in his own words, five months before Hugh Byas's report of a formal independence proclamation (Jones; Byas).

Jones is a valuable eyewitness but, like Haslund-Christensen elsewhere in this entry, needs to be read with some care: he was a 1935 Western outsider whose interview passed through several layers of translation — Mongolian to Chinese to German to English — so the exact wording of anything attributed to Demchugdongrub should be treated as approximate rather than verbatim, and his account carries some of the condescension typical of the era's travel writing. His direct physical observations and the substance of the recorded interview are treated here as the reliable core; his age guess and similar asides are treated more cautiously.

Contemporary Western observers reached a similar assessment independently. The American Sinologist Owen Lattimore, writing in 1935 after direct contact with Mongol autonomy activists, identified Demchugdongrub — "of West Sunid, in Silingol League" — as the movement's best-known leader, and, dismissing rumors that he sought to become a second Chinggis Khan, likened him instead to "an Inner Mongolian Roosevelt," steering a middle course between the movement's more radical and more cautious factions toward a pragmatic autonomy (Lattimore, "Wickedness"). Lattimore also linked the movement's timing directly to the 1932 founding of Manchukuo — see the [Mongolian Political Affairs Council](/entry/mongolian-political-affairs-council) entry for that argument in full.

Independent, incidental confirmation of his movements in this period comes from Western travellers' accounts of [Hohhot](/entry/hohhot) collected by the scholar Isabelle Charleux. She notes that Demchugdongrub — described there as the "country's new master" — occasionally resided at a Mongol encampment pitched outside the city in the mid-1930s, drawing on the Danish ethnographer Henning Haslund-Christensen's account of the period alongside the American writer Graham Peck's separate observations of Mongol princes' general presence in the city (Charleux). This is corroborating context, not a major claim about his activities.

Haslund-Christensen is a valuable firsthand observer of 1930s Hohhot — he had lived among Mongols for years and traveled with Sven Hedin's expeditions — but Charleux's research shows him to be unreliable on historical attribution specifically: touring a Hohhot temple with Demchugdongrub himself, Haslund-Christensen credited its foundation to Altan Khan, an attribution Charleux identifies as mistaken (Charleux). His eyewitness observations, including the one above about Demchugdongrub's residence, are treated here as more trustworthy than any claims he makes about the deeper past.

## Mengjiang: founding and leadership

Japanese interest in Demchugdongrub predates [Mengjiang](/entry/mengjiang) itself. Kwantung Army headquarters drafted a policy for "guiding the Mongols" as early as July 1933, and Tokyo's Zenrin Kyokai ("Friendly Neighbor Society") began organizing among Inner Mongolians from January 1934 (Zorigt, "Cooperation with the Japanese"). Japanese intelligence officers courted him directly over the following two years: Doihara Kenji, the Kwantung Army intelligence chief later convicted as a Class A war criminal, met with him in November 1934, and Itagaki Seishiro — another future war-crimes convict — met with him and Prince Sodnamrabdan in September 1935 to discuss Kwantung Army backing for a Mongol state (Zorigt, "Cooperation with the Japanese"). Commercial courtship followed a similar logic: also in 1935, a representative of the Manchuria Aviation Company, Nagabuchi Saburō, traveled to Pailingmiao to press Demchugdongrub to expand the company's operations and buy aircraft, reportedly telling him that "in this age of the airplane, the future lies in transportation across the sky, so the country with the most airplanes will master the globe" — an appeal to the same Chinggis Khan-descended, nomad-conquest imagery Demchugdongrub cultivated elsewhere, which the company's own magazine recorded him as "exceptionally pleased" by (Ambaras and McDonald, "New Nomads of the Sky," citing the company magazine *ManAir*, 1935). A photograph dated to around this time, held via Wikimedia Commons, shows Demchugdongrub together with [Li Shouxin](/entry/li-shouxin) and the Japanese officer Tanaka Ryūkichi (Ambaras and McDonald, "New Nomads of the Sky") — its licensing has not been checked for use in this atlas. That December, Demchugdongrub flew to the Manchukuo capital at Changchun, met Kwantung Army commander Minami Jiro, and paid respects to the Manchukuo emperor Puyi, who, on Japanese advice, granted him an honorific princely title (Zorigt, "Cooperation with the Japanese").

Around the same time, the *New York Times* reported — via its Tokyo bureau, relaying Japanese press dispatches, a filter worth keeping in mind when weighing its framing — that Demchugdongrub had proclaimed the independence of western Inner Mongolia around December 22, 1935, summoning thirty-three chiefs and claiming a territory of about two million people (Byas). No other source used in this atlas describes a formal independence proclamation at this specific moment, which fell between his December visit to Manchukuo and the February 1936 Mongolian Army General Headquarters ceremony described below. It came five months after he had already described a pan-Mongol unification aim to Gareth Jones in July 1935 (see above), so the December reports, however imprecisely sourced, are consistent with ambitions he had stated openly earlier that year.

Mongol self-rule under Demchugdongrub then passed through several Japanese-sponsored reorganizations. Zorigt's account gives February 12, 1936 as the date a "[Mongolian Army General Headquarters](/entry/inner-mongolian-army)" was inaugurated at his own palace, using the Chinggis Khan calendar and a new blue banner in place of Republic of China symbols; a first Mongolian Congress followed at Ujumuchin on April 24, 1936, debating a full Mongol state spanning Inner and Outer Mongolia and Kokonor — the same union of Mongol territories Demchugdongrub had described to Gareth Jones the previous July — before settling on the more modest [Mongolian Military Government](/entry/mongolian-military-government), formally established at Dehua on May 12, 1936 with Demchugdongrub at its head (Zorigt, "Cooperation with the Japanese"; Jagchid). A Mongol attempt to seize Suiyuan from Chinese forces that November ended in defeat at Bat-khaalag itself. After full-scale war broke out between China and Japan in July 1937, Demchugdongrub's forces took Guisui (Hohhot) that October, and a second Mongolian Congress there on October 27, 1937 created the [Mongolian Allied League Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-league-autonomous-government), with the elderly Yondonwangchug as nominal chairman and Demchugdongrub as vice chairman and de facto head of administration (Zorigt, "Cooperation with the Japanese"). The government was formally inaugurated in Hohhot on December 1, 1937, with Demchugdongrub heading its Department of Administration and the more Japanese-aligned Li Shouxin as military commander (Jagchid). It was around the same time, with the formation of a Japanese-run "Joint Committee of Mongolian Territories" that November unifying this Mongol government with puppet regimes in Southern Chahar and Northern Shanxi, that the name "Mengjiang" entered common use for the combined territory (Jagchid). After Yondonwangchug's death in March 1938, Demchugdongrub was elected chairman outright by a Third Mongolian Congress on July 1, 1938 (Jagchid). That government gave way in September 1939 to the [Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-autonomous-government), which continued under Demchugdongrub until August 1941 (Jagchid). It was during these two years — when, in Jagchid's account, Demchugdongrub kept the title of chairman but lost real power to the Japanese advisor Kanai Shoji — that the prince secretly opened contact with Chiang Kai-shek's wartime capital at Chungking and, with [Li Shouxin](/entry/li-shouxin), explored defecting from Japanese-held territory before the plot was uncovered; see the [Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-autonomous-government) entry for that episode, which complicates any picture of him as a straightforward Japanese puppet.

The final and best-known iteration, the [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state) (Mongolian: Monggol-un Obesuben Jasakhu Ulus), was inaugurated on August 4, 1941 in [Kalgan](/entry/kalgan), with Demchugdongrub continuing as chairman (Jagchid). Jagchid describes this as Demchugdongrub's "second golden era," though one that "came too late" — real financial, police, and transport power in Mengjiang remained largely in Japanese hands throughout, administered through a rotating cast of Japanese "supreme advisors," and Demchugdongrub spent much of the period maneuvering against Japanese officials (notably Kanai Shoji) who sought to keep the Mongol government subordinate.

## After 1945: capture, imprisonment, and death

Accounts of Demchugdongrub's final two decades come from sources that agree on the broad outline but differ in emphasis and some detail. Following Japan's defeat in August 1945, he attempted to keep leading resistance from the west, sending his family into Soviet-Mongolian custody while he tried to rally scattered Mongol forces; in 1949 he led one final government, the [Mongolian Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-government-alashan), based in Alashan (Jagchid; Zorigt, "Fate of His Family"). At the end of December 1949 he crossed into the Mongolian People's Republic, reportedly believing Marshal Choibalsan wanted to discuss a joint push toward unifying Inner and Outer Mongolia (Zorigt, "Fate of His Family"). Instead he was held, interrogated for months by Mongolian and Soviet officials, and formally arrested on February 27, 1950 as an alleged enemy of the state (Zorigt, "Fate of His Family"). He was extradited to Beijing on September 18, 1950 (Jagchid), a date Zorigt's account places more vaguely as "late 1950."

Set against this account is a run of contemporaneous US intelligence field reports that tells a materially different story and, on the central question of when and whether Demchugdongrub was captured, flatly contradicts it. These are CIA field reports of the late 1940s and early 1950s: granular and contemporaneous, but unevaluated informant reporting, often relayed through Nationalist-aligned channels and anti-Communist in orientation, with troop figures that are partisan estimates rather than counts — one report calls the higher numbers "unduly high." They are weighed here below the atlas's scholarly and memoir sources, but they are too specific and too numerous to set aside. By their account Demchugdongrub spent the postwar years not in custody but leading anti-Communist Mongol resistance in the Ningxia–Suiyuan–Alashan borderlands.

The refusals came first. In June 1947 Fu Zuoyi telegraphed asking him to return to Inner Mongolia with promises of cooperation and help toward autonomy; Demchugdongrub refused, replying that the proposal "had come one year too late" (Source U). His distrust ran deeper than tactics: a March 1949 report has him, before leaving Beiping, rebuffing requests from the Soviet consulate and from Ulanhu's eastern government to remain and cooperate, reminding the Communist representatives that his family were already prisoners of the Soviets in the Mongolian People's Republic and that, had he been willing to cooperate, he would have done so to free them (Source S) — a stance of family-rooted distrust that complicates, without disproving, Zorigt's account of a hopeful December 1949 crossing to discuss unification.

Through 1948–49 he sought Nationalist sanction to move west and lead the Northwest Mongols "on the same basis as Fu Zuoyi and the Ma brothers": the government at first did not reply, possibly blocked by CC-Clique Mongols in the Mongolian-Tibetan Affairs Commission (Source H, December 1948), but approval was subsequently granted (Source I, January 1949). At Lanzhou in January–February 1949 he pressed for autonomy and weapons and received only a deputy's verbal approval and, in the report's phrase, "nothing concrete" (Source C). His forces built toward perhaps six thousand by mid-1949 — around two thousand cavalry that had fought their way out of Manchuria, described as the only organized Nationalist force to escape, plus some fifteen hundred already in Suiyuan and Chahar and a possible further two thousand under a commander named Hotoringga (Source J) — even as he failed to obtain Central Government funds and feared his unpaid cavalry would "resort to banditry" (Source K). All the while his representatives hedged leftward, in contact in Shanghai and Guangzhou with the China Democratic League and the Kuomintang Revolutionary Committee — including Luo Longji — who pledged support and cooperation "in any coalition government" (Sources V and W), consistent with the secret Chungking contacts recorded above. Nationalist-aligned Hong Kong press separately reported material and psychological backing for his resistance and had his representatives meeting Wang Shijie in Taiwan in 1950, though that channel carries an obvious partisan slant (Source X).

It is the reporting from 1950 onward that cannot be squared with Zorigt's dating. Where Zorigt has Demchugdongrub arrested in the Mongolian People's Republic on February 27, 1950, the field reports place him free and militarily active for two more years: raiding Ningxia from an Alashan base in early 1950 (Source L); sending representatives that February to negotiate a rapprochement with the local Chinese Communist authorities in Ningxia, an analyst judging him "in desperate circumstances" and probably "seeking a local tactical advantage" (Source M); commanding perhaps twenty thousand men by August 1950 after a defected Fu Zuoyi force joined him, his representatives giving "a flat refusal" to a Communist negotiating mission (Source N); still resisting overtures — in May 1950 the Chinese Communists sent [Bai Haifeng](/entry/bai-haifeng) and He Zhaolin to try to win him over, without success (Source D); his forces reported at around ten thousand in mid-1951, with Fu Zuoyi cavalry defecting to him rather than deploy to Korea (Source P), and mounting a two-day engagement in Alashan before withdrawing to Ejine Torghut (Source Q); and, as late as June 1952, living as a nomad off his herds near the Suiyuan–Ningxia–Mongolian People's Republic tripoint with former Ma Hongkui troops (Source R). One December 1950 report offers a partial bridge, describing his forces as by then fragmented into small scattered units with Demchugdongrub himself "in the desert area" and only a few followers (Source O) — a mechanism by which a rump resistance might have operated under his name even as his own position collapsed. But it does not reconcile first-hand 1951–52 sightings of him free and commanding troops with an arrest in early 1950. This atlas records the conflict between Zorigt's archival dating and the CIA field reporting and does not resolve it; the troop numbers throughout are wildly varying informant estimates, not counts.

A third voice adds to this conflict without resolving it. The edited scholarly volume *Imperial Japan and National Identities in Asia, 1895–1945* gives what might be called the orthodox postwar account: that Demchugdongrub fled to Outer Mongolia and was later jailed by the Chinese communist government, where he was made to confess that his wartime actions were "traitorous" (attribution to a specific chapter and page not verified — this atlas holds only a pasted excerpt of the volume, not the full text; see this atlas's project notes). This scholarly synthesis broadly tracks Zorigt's account in outline — flight to Mongolia, then arrest and confinement in China — rather than the CIA field reports' picture of continued free militancy into 1951–52, and is recorded here as a further, independent-seeming voice on the Zorigt side of that conflict, not as a tiebreaker; the volume is itself partly downstream of Jagchid for material of this kind, so its independence from this atlas's other sources is not fully established.

In the account Jagchid and Zorigt follow, once in China he was held as a war criminal, reportedly sent to labor at the Fushun coal mines, and — per Jagchid — released under a general amnesty on April 9, 1963 after roughly twelve and a half years in custody, after which accounts vary as to whether he lived under surveillance, worked in an institute library, or remained under informal house arrest in Hohhot. The two sources disagree on what happened to his family: Zorigt's account describes his wife and children being arrested in Mongolia the day after his own arrest and then sent into internal exile within Mongolia, while Jagchid states that during his imprisonment his family (except his eldest son, whose fate Jagchid says was unknown to him) were sent from Mongolia back into China. Both agree his household suffered serious hardship as a result of his fate.

The gap Jagchid leaves open — the eldest son's fate — can be partly filled from the CIA field reports, though only partly, and this is the atlas's own cross-source linkage rather than any single source's claim. Three of the reports independently place Demchugdongrub's eldest son — whom the garbled originals render "Te Hua," also "Yugar-Jap" — a prisoner in the Mongolian People's Republic in 1945–47: captured with the family at the Japanese collapse and sent alone to a middle school in Ulan Bator (Source F), and still in the republic as of early 1947, with Demchugdongrub then unable to locate his family (Source T); the [1945 provisional government](/entry/inner-mongolian-republican-provisional-government) entry notes the same capture. This corroborates the underlying fact that the eldest son was held in the Mongolian People's Republic, filling Jagchid's gap, even as it does not fix his name: the identification of this son with Dugarsuren (also Dugursulong), named in later family sources, is this atlas's inference and is asserted by none of the reports themselves.

His death date is also unsettled. Jagchid's biography states outright that three different dates have been reported for it — April 25, May 23, and June 4, 1966 — and that he could not determine which was correct, though he leans toward May 23, 1966 as the date most commonly cited, placing the death in Hohhot at the start of the Chinese Cultural Revolution. Zorigt's account simply states that he "died in 1966" without specifying a date. Jagchid reports that Demchugdongrub's ashes were later scattered on the mountain that had once held the oboo (ceremonial cairn) of the Sunid Right Banner. Jagchid adds that many of his accomplishments were destroyed by the Cultural Revolution, and that [Khafungga](/entry/khafungga) and most of his other compatriots who had struggled for Mongolian nationalistic aspirations — a very different, Communist-aligned strand of that struggle in Khafungga's case — did not escape persecution during that period either (Jagchid).

His wife's name is also given differently across sources, and this entry does not attempt to resolve it. Jagchid's biography names her Sebjidma, both in the narrative text and in the book's own index (Jagchid, p. 447). Zorigt's account of the family's fate instead lists her as Renchintsoo. This is not a case of one source simply using a title where the other uses a name: khatan is a rank-word for a consort or princess rather than a personal name on its own, but "Renchintsoo" is given as a name in addition to that rank, not instead of it. It is possible both names are genuine — referring to different wives, or to the same woman at different points in her life — or that one source is simply mistaken; nothing in either source settles the question, so both are recorded here rather than one being silently preferred.

Independent of this specific naming disagreement, Hedin's 1929 account of the palace household is evidence that plural marriage was practiced in this family, which makes "more than one wife" a documented pattern here rather than pure speculation. Hedin records two dowager queens then living in Sünid, one of them Demchugdongrub's mother, and states that Demchugdongrub's father had had several wives; he separately describes Demchugdongrub's own wife at that time as having recently borne an heir after losing four earlier children, and as living in seclusion (Hedin). This does not resolve which name, if either, belongs to which wife, or whether Sebjidma and Renchintsoo are the same person — it only shows that the household is not a case where a single-wife assumption should be taken for granted.
