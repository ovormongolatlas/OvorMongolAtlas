Hohhot — known to Mongols as Kökeqota ("blue city") and to Chinese as Guihuacheng — was, through the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, a cosmopolitan Mongol trading and religious center rather than simply a Chinese administrative town (Charleux). It grew up as a paired, or "twin," city: the old Mongol town (the "Old City") sat beside the separate Qing dynasty garrison of Suiyuan (the "New City"), built under the Qianlong Emperor in the 1730s, and the two retained distinct identities for generations even though they stood only a few kilometers apart (Charleux). In 1913 the new Republic of China government administratively unified Guihua and Suiyuan into a single city, Guisui (Charleux).

## Caravan hub and colonization

For centuries Hohhot served as a gateway to the Mongol plateau: expeditions bound for Uliastai, Kobdo, Xinjiang, or the Gobi routinely assembled there, hiring guides and buying camels and horses before setting out. Charleux quotes Owen Lattimore's observation that the town's grain and other supplies made it "a natural centre of Mongol trade and a nodal point of caravan traffic," a characterization she pairs with Sven Hedin's associate Henning Haslund-Christensen's description of it as the natural terminus where Mongolia's caravan traffic met China (Charleux). The opening of a rail line to Hohhot in 1921 — alongside electricity and telephone service — changed this rapidly. Where the journey from Beijing had once taken roughly two weeks, the railway now brought a wave of Chinese settlers from Shanxi, Shaanxi, and Hebei who came to dominate the city demographically; the American scholar John DeFrancis counted about 120,000 residents in 1935, most of them Chinese, with the native Tümed Mongols reduced to a minority presence even though monks and nobles still lived within the walls and most other Mongols camped in yurts around the city's edges (Charleux).
