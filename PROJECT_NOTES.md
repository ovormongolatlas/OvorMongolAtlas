# Övör Mongol Atlas — Project Notes

Internal working reference. Read this before editing entries. It records the project's methodology, what is settled versus uncertain, and how each source should be handled. Not a public website page.

## Scope
An interactive historical atlas of Inner Mongolia (Övör Mongol), late Qing to early PRC, centered on Demchugdongrub (Prince De / Te Wang), the West Sünit ruling house, and the Mengjiang regime. Value comes from careful source criticism, not from copying general references.

## Confidence tiers (apply to every claim)
- Tier 1, Solid: supported by multiple independent sources and/or solid scholarship. State plainly, with citation.
- Tier 2, Attributed: a single scholarly/contemporary source, or a family/partisan source. State with explicit attribution ("according to X").
- Tier 3, Tentative: uncited compilations, unresolved conflicts, or machine-drafted script. Flag explicitly as unverified.

## Sourcing rules
- Paraphrase in your own words. Direct quotes under ~15 words, one per source, always attributed.
- Never commit raw source PDFs or article text. The `research/` folder is gitignored for raw sources.
- Record conflicting sources rather than harmonizing them. Distinguish primary from secondary. Preserve and explain transliterations. Flag disputed facts.
- Facts with three independent witnesses can be stated plainly. Single-source or conflicting facts must be attributed or flagged.

## Settled (Tier 1) facts
- Born 1902 (specific date 8 February 1902 per Chinese administrative records), in Sünit (Sonid) Right Banner, which is the same as West Sünit, in Shilingol (Silingol) League.
- Hereditary prince (Zasag, Wang rank) of West Sünit; the line claimed Chinggisid Borjigin descent. Corroborated by Hedin ("Barun Sünit Wang"), Jones (1935), and scholarship.
- Seat at Wangin Ortu, a Chinese-style palace with a private temple (Hedin and Jones, both eyewitnesses).
- Led the Inner Mongolian autonomy movement from the 1930s.
- Pailingmiao (Bailingmiao) autonomy council inaugurated 23 April 1934; 28 members; Nanking-funded at $50,000/month (Abend, NYT 1934).
- Proclaimed independence of western Inner Mongolia c. 22 December 1935 (Byas, NYT 1935, filed via Japanese dispatches).
- May 1936: Nanking ordered him to dissolve the council and leave Pailingmiao; Japanese executions of Hsingan Mongol leaders had estranged him from Japan; his alignment was genuinely uncertain (NYT 1936).
- Mengjiang founding sequence (Tuo 2019): 1936, Mongol military government in Sünit Right Banner; 27 October 1937, Mongolia Federation Autonomous Government in Hohhot (after the 7 July 1937 Marco Polo Bridge Incident); 22 November 1937, Mengjiang Federation Committee at Kalgan uniting three puppet governments (South Chahar/Kalgan, North Jin/Datong, Mongolia Federation/Hohhot); 1 September 1939, merged into the single Mengjiang Government.
- Suiyuan campaign, November 1936, defeated; Bailingmiao retaken late December 1936.
- Mengjiang demographics (Tuo, citing Japanese records): ~5.21M Han, 290,000 Mongols, 94,000 Hui (1939); ~4.86M Han, 300,000 Mongols (1941). Mongols under 6 percent. Central irony: a nominally Mongol state overwhelmingly Han.
- Post-1945: imprisoned by the PRC, held at Zhangjiakou from 1950, pardoned and released 1963, died 1966 (Narangoa; Chinese records).
- His eldest son was executed in the Mongolian People's Republic and later rehabilitated (execution confirmed by Narangoa; name and dates from family sources, see open questions).

## Open questions (do NOT state as settled)
- Wife's name: Jagchid (The Last Mongol Prince, p. 447) says Sebjidma; Zorigt says Renchintsoo. Hedin attests plural marriage in the household (his father was polygamous; two dowagers plus his own wife). Possibly two wives, or one name is a title ("khatan" is a rank-word). Unresolved.
- Eldest son: "Dugursulong" (Chinese blog, executed 1951) / "Dugarsüren" (sonin.mn family memorial, sentenced 1952, executed at 35) / "Dugarsuren" (fan chart, 1917–1951). Likely the same person; name spelling and death year unresolved.
- Unnamed younger brother in the Joel Eriksson photo (c. 1930–1938): identity unknown; caption as unidentified.
- Other children's identities and exact dates: only from the uncited fan chart and the family memorial. Tier 3.
- Founding dates vary slightly across sources (e.g., 27 vs 28 October 1937). Record, do not harmonize.
- Li Shouxin's origins conflict: Narangerel has him rise through Zhang Xueliang's Northeastern Army; Jagchid tells a monastery-boy → Chinese-bandit-chief ("Big Xin") → Japanese-promoted-in-1933 story, and disputes the name origin too. Recorded in his entry as two accounts, not harmonized.

## Source reliability (per source)
- Jagchid, The Last Mongol Prince (1999): scholarly, high reliability. Wife as Sebjidma (p. 447); flag symbolism (p. 219). OPEN ACCESS — published free/open by Western Washington University's Western CEDAR (East Asian Studies Press), so it is quotable and safe to use, not an in-copyright scanned book. A full searchable text has been read; page numbers can be cited. Caveat: Jagchid is a participant-partisan (father was Lubsangchoijur, a Gungsangnorbu man and Wu Heling backer; Jagchid was related to Wu by marriage, later Wu's secretary and rival, then secretary to Prince Sungjingwangchug in the 1939–41 Allied Autonomous Government). Weight his eyewitness detail highly but treat his character judgments (e.g. of Li Shouxin) as attributed opinion.
- Tuo Jianing, Literature and Modern China 1/1 (2019), DOI 10.54591/CQUN2903: scholarly, critical, high reliability. Founding chronology, demographics. Open access.
- Isabelle Charleux, Mongolica Pragensia '17, 10/1 (2020), HAL halshs-02984229: scholarly. Hohhot. Open access.
- Owen Lattimore, "On the Wickedness of Being Nomads" (T'ien Hsia Monthly, Aug 1935) and "The Geographical Factor in Mongol History" (Geographical Journal, Jan 1938): named contemporary scholar. Source of the "Inner Mongolian Roosevelt" characterization (1935) and, from a 1936 lecture, "tied hand and foot and thrown to the Japanese." In-copyright/access-restricted.
- Li Narangoa / "End of Empire" (ANU): scholarly. Post-1945 fate.
- New York Times: Hallett Abend (27 May 1934), Hugh Byas (24 Dec 1935), unsigned (1 May 1936). Primary contemporary journalism; valuable for dating and framing. Byas 1935 filed via Japanese dispatches (framing caveat). In-copyright.
- Sven Hedin, Riddles of the Gobi Desert: named contemporary eyewitness (1929). Reliable for what he saw (title, palace, plural marriage). Local copy is OCR, so verify wording before quoting. Separate observation from inference.
- Gareth Jones, letter of 14 July 1935 (garethjones.org): eyewitness; strong for the palace and De Wang's stated pan-Mongol aim. But an outsider, interview passed through several translation layers, era condescension, and he guessed De Wang's age at 45–50 when he was 33. Estate copyright.
- Henning Haslund-Christensen: valuable firsthand observer, but shown by Charleux to be unreliable on historical attribution. Weight his eyewitness observations over his claims about the deeper past.
- Alfred Lückenhaus, "Mengchiang: A Country in the Making," The XXth Century (Shanghai), 1943: PROPAGANDA. Written by a German DNB (Nazi news agency) correspondent for a pro-Axis Shanghai magazine, about a Japanese puppet state. Use ONLY as evidence of the regime's self-image and for cross-checkable administrative detail, always explicitly attributed, never as plain fact.
- Zorigt (Mongolian articles): family-fate account; names wife Renchintsoo. Family/partisan perspective.
- sonin.mn memorial (2018): a family memorial (öchil) by a descendant, addressed to the eldest son Dugarsüren. Partisan.
- Fan family-tree chart ("The Royal Family of Mengjiang"): uncited enthusiast compilation with colorized/possibly AI portraits. Research lead only; everything from it is Tier 3.
- David Ambaras and Kate McDonald, *Bodies and Structures 2.0: Deep-Mapping Modern East Asian History* (Scalar, NEH-funded): scholarly born-digital project. Used for two pages so far — "New Nomads of the Sky" (1935 Manchuria Aviation Company courtship of Demchugdongrub, relaying the company's own magazine *ManAir*) and "The View from Above" (1940 Japanese aerial-photography feature on Mengjiang, citing *Chiri kyōiku*). High reliability; treat its own citations to primary Japanese-era magazines as secondhand until checked directly.

## Structural decisions
- Entry types: person, place, group, battle, state (a dedicated "lineage" type is optional).
- The West Sünit lineage page is titled "The ruling house of West Sünit Banner." It is NOT a "dynasty" and NOT "the royal house of Mengjiang." Structure it from solid core to tentative genealogy.
- Mengjiang is a sequence of successive bodies (1936 military government, 1937 federation, 1939 merged government), not a single founding. Describe it plainly and neutrally as a Japanese-backed puppet state. Pair the Lückenhaus propaganda (regime self-image) against Tuo's critical scholarship (reality).
- Multi-script names: schema fields for Mongol bichig, romanized Mongolian, Chinese, and common English. Each optional per entry; display only what exists. Mark each name as sourced or converter-drafted. Bichig needs a Mongolian webfont plus vertical rendering; the Kimo converter is a drafting aid whose output must be verified.
- Site pages built or planned: About, Editorial Principles, client-side search.
