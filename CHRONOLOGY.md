# OvorMongolAtlas — Master Event Chronology (working spine)

Internal working reference, companion to [PROJECT_NOTES.md](PROJECT_NOTES.md). Not a public website page. Maintainer-supplied; added to the repo verbatim in substance during the "chronology + multi-entry pass" (see PROJECT_NOTES for that pass's log).

Purpose: the ordering layer for the atlas, i.e. what happened in what sequence, so entries can hang off a shared timeline. This is not the visual timeline/map. Each line is `date — event [source(s)] {flags}`. Source conflicts are recorded side by side, never harmonized. Where a claim rests on one informant or a weak source, it is tagged.

## Do-not-merge reminder
Two uploads (Winstanley-Chesters & Cathcart; and Dudin's milieu) cite **James Boyd** (2002, *Japanese Studies*, "In Pursuit of an Obsession"). That is a different scholar from **John Hunter Boyle** (1972). Keep them distinct everywhere, as already flagged in PROJECT_NOTES. (Note for this atlas: the previously-cited Boyd article in this repo is his 2013 "The Army that Never Was" — a second, separate Boyd publication from the 2002 one named in this chronology's uploads; both are James Boyd, still distinct from Boyle.)

## The connective thread
One grievance recurs under different names across the whole spine: Chinese agricultural **land reclamation** on banner pasture (放垦 / 開墾 / *kaihuang*). It drives the Jindandao massacre (1891), the encroachment that produced the Hua Teh/Jabsar renaming, the Gada Meiren uprising (1929–31), the anti-Manchukuo folk songs, and then reappears with inverted political valence in the CCP's 1947–52 reclamation drive that Ulanhu fought to stop. Worth carrying as a theme entry.

## Era 0 — Precursors (pre-1912)

- **1891, Nov–Dec** — Jindandao (金丹道) incident: Han secret-society revolt across Josotu and Juu Uda leagues massacres Mongols (casualty estimates wildly contested, ~150,000–500,000); suppressed late Dec. Strengthens Mongol nationalism and prompts Beijing to route Mongol-Han disputes and tax collection through Chinese authorities, weakening banners. [reference — verify; scholarly: Borjigin Burensain] {casualty figure UNSETTLED; flag as precursor at the early edge of scope}
- **1898** — Gungsangnorbu succeeds Prince Vangdudnamjil as 12th and last prince (jasag) of Kharachin Right Banner, Josotu League. [reference — verify against Jagchid 1979]
- **1902** — Gungsangnorbu founds one of the first modern schools in Inner Mongolia (Chongzheng school). [reference — verify against Jagchid 1979]
- **1903** — Gungsangnorbu visits Meiji Japan with Manchu nobles; on return founds a military school and a girls' school, both with Japanese teachers; later sends students to Japan. [reference — verify against Jagchid 1979]

## Era 1 — Early Republic: autonomy bargaining and encroachment (1912–1928)

- **1912, Aug** — Beijing's "Treatment of Mongols Ordinance" (蒙古待遇条例): preserves princely ranks and the banner "right of jurisdiction," keeps the league-banner system, to stop princes defecting to newly independent Outer Mongolia. [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1912** — Third Russo-Japanese Entente: Russia recognized in Northern (Outer) Mongolia, Japan's "special interest" acknowledged in eastern Inner Mongolia. [Narangoa 2004, p.49]
- **1911–1913** — Gungsangnorbu leans royalist at the Xinhai Revolution, attempts a Bogd Khan / Pan-Mongol alliance (fails on Han-population reality and factionalism), turns to Japanese backing; Japanese army dispatches liaison officers (Dec 1911); arms-buying via the Kawashima Naniwa network is intercepted. [reference — verify against Jagchid 1979]
- **1913** — Gungsangnorbu secures approval for the Mongolian-Tibetan School (蒙藏学校) in Beijing — the institution that trains the young Mongols who later feed the IMPRP. [reference — verify against Jagchid 1979] {this is the hinge between Gungsangnorbu and the 1920s radicals}
- **1914** — Beijing sets up special administrative regions over the banner areas; **1914 Jul** the Dutong-office system (都統署官制) creates Rehe (Juu Uda, Josotu), Chahar (Chahar part, Shilingol), Suiyuan (Ulanqab, Ikh Juu, Tümed) special regions. [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1915** — Twenty-One Demands: Japan's first official claim to special rights in "Eastern Inner Mongolia"; the term Man-Mō (Manchuria-Mongolia) enters Japanese media and by the 1920s is framed as Japan's "lifeline." [Narangoa 2004, pp.49–50]
- **1924** — Mongol Affairs Conference (蒙事会議): princes split between keeping the league system and converting leagues to provinces to resist Han encroachment. [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1925** — Inner Mongolian People's Revolutionary Party (IMPRP) founded (Zhangjiakou); Merse (Guo Daofu) among the leaders. {NOT in the five uploads; source from the repo's Merse material / Atwood; placed here as a node} {SOURCE NEEDED}
- **1928** — Nanjing upgrades the three special regions to full provinces (Rehe, Chahar, Suiyuan); leagues and provinces now overlap, which Mongols read as territorial dissolution. [Hirokawa 2024; Narangoa 2004]

## Era 2 — Reclamation crisis and Gada Meiren (1929–1933)

- **1929** — Northeast warlord administration (under Zhang Xueliang's Fengtien regime) colludes with banner authorities to sell/reclaim ~three-quarters of Darhan Banner (Khorchin Left Wing Middle Banner, Jirim) grassland to Han settlers. Gada Meiren (personal name Nadmid; a *meiren*, i.e. banner militia officer) leads resistance, then armed uprising. [reference — verify; scholarly: Borjigin Burensain 2003, "遼北荒" reclamation-and-uprising study]
- **1931, ~Apr 4–5** — Gada Meiren surrounded at the Shar Mörön (Xar Moron) river by a Fengtian force **led by Li Shouxin (Buyandelger)** and killed; uprising disperses. [reference — verify] {LINKS DIRECTLY to the Li Shouxin entry. The detailed suppression narrative in the repo traces to Li Shouxin's 1985 memoir via Narangerel Pt.2 — tag as SINGLE-SOURCE and PERPETRATOR-ACCOUNT}
- **1931, Sept** — Mukden (Manchurian) Incident halts the Fengtien land-survey/Liaobei project. [Narangoa 2004; Gada Meiren refs]
- **1932, Mar** — Manchukuo founded; Mongols of Manchukuo administered via the Xing'an Office (initially "special status," progressively curtailed from 1937). [Narangoa 2004, pp.62–64] Manchukuo adopts a "Mongolian land-protection policy," aborting the Chinese colonization projects — which spares Darhan Banner from abolition. [Gada Meiren refs — verify]
- **1933, Mar** — Japan occupies Rehe (Jehol); the Juu Uda and Josotu leagues' banner system is abolished and folded into Rehe/Jinzhou provinces; local Mongols resent it. [Hirokawa 2024; Narangoa 2004, p.52]

## Era 3 — Autonomy movement and the Japanese approach (1933–1936)

- **1934, Feb** — Nanjing adopts the "Eight Principles for Mongol Local Autonomy" (蒙古地方自治辦法原則八項), permitting a Mongol autonomy council. [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1934, Apr** — the Mongol Local Autonomy Political Council (蒙政会) is inaugurated at Pailingmiao/Bailingmiao; De Wang (Demchugdongrub, prince of **Sünit Right Banner, Shilingol** — the West Sünit house) is Secretary-General; region declared formally autonomous. [Narangoa 2004, p.63; Boyle 1972, p.124; Hirokawa 2024] {naming reconciliation already in PROJECT_NOTES}
- **1934 (month contested: Mar / May / Sep)** — Chahar province creates a new *hsien* named Hua Teh (化德) at Jabsar (嘉卜寺), split from Kangbao and Shangdu, on banner land; the encroachment that becomes the Hua Teh↔Teh Hua affair. [Boyle 1972, p.125, wordplay via Hangin p.38, SINGLE-SOURCE; Chinese local histories for the 設治局 founding] {FOUNDING MONTH UNSETTLED even among Chinese sources; distinct from the April 1934 Bailingmiao Council above — do not conflate}
- **1935, late** — Japanese army invades eastern Chahar (察東事件); by Oct 1935 Doihara Kenji has convinced De Wang there is no conflict between Mongol autonomy and Kwantung Army aims. [Boyle 1972, p.125; Hirokawa 2024] {Boyle's Oct-1935 Doihara summary vs the repo's existing Nov-1934 Zorigt date: likely two different contacts, not a contradiction — keep both, flagged}
- **1936, Jan** — Chahar League Office (察哈爾盟公署) set up at Zhangbei under the 蒙政会 name (Japanese-sponsored). [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1936, Feb** — Nanjing sets up a rival Suiyuan-border Mongol Political Council (綏境蒙政会); the Bailingmiao council splits (formalized Jul 1936). [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1936, Feb** — De Wang establishes the Mongol Army General HQ (蒙古軍総司令部) in **Sünit Right Banner** — the West Sünit node of the military movement. [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1936, Apr / May** — the Mongol Military Government (蒙古軍政府) is established at Dehua (德化, the renamed Hua Teh/Jabsar); Yun Wang (Yondonwangchug) chairman, De Wang director-general (zongcai). Referred to as the Suiyuan Incident (綏遠事変). [Hirokawa 2024 gives Apr; Chinese sources give the founding as **12 May 1936**; Narangoa 2004 p.64 cites a Manchukuo root-policy act of **20 May 1936**] {THREE dates in play — see conflicts section; they are not all the same event}

## Era 4 — Mengjiang formation (1937–1939)

- **1937, Jul** — Second Sino-Japanese War begins; from 1937 Japan also cuts back the Xing'an Office's authority in Manchukuo. [Narangoa 2004, p.64]
- **1937, Aug–Oct** — Chahar operation; **Sep 4** South Chahar Autonomous Government (Kalgan); **Oct 15** North Shansi Autonomous Government (Tatung); Suiyuan taken in Oct. [Boyle 1972, p.127]
- **1937, Oct 27–29** — Federated Autonomous Government of Mongolia (蒙古聯盟自治政府 lineage; Boyle's "Federated Autonomous Government of Mongolia") formed from the Mongol princes' convocation; headed by Yun Wang, capital eventually Hohhot. [Boyle 1972, p.127]
- **1937, Nov 22** — United Committee of Mengchiang (蒙疆聯合委員会 / Mökyö Rengö Iinkai) established — Boyle's "United Committee." [Boyle 1972, pp.132–133; Hirokawa 2024] {THIS is the entity the repo's earlier pass could not place against the repo's "Joint Committee"/"Mengjiang Federation Committee" — see genealogy below}
- **1937, Dec** — Baotou occupied; the Mongol League Autonomous Government (蒙古聯盟自治政府) set up, capital Hohhot (厚和); Suiyuan and Chahar provinces abolished and reorganized into new leagues: **Chahar, Shilingol, Ulanqab, Bayantala**, plus Hohhot and Baotou cities. [Hirokawa 2024] {the Ulanqab/Bayantala reorganization is Hirokawa's core contribution; Bayantala absorbs Wuchuan & Guyang counties from old Ulanqab, which becomes the grievance at the 1940 conference}
- **1938–1939** — Tada Fumio + Keijo Imperial University geographic fieldwork/expedition into Mengjiang (surveys of markets, pastoral society, Hohhot). [Winstanley-Chesters & Cathcart 2022] {peripheral; a knowledge-making data point, useful for a "how Mengjiang was mapped" theme}
- **1938, Aug** — the League Office System (盟公署官制) is promulgated and the Mengjiang United Committee strengthened; leagues reorganized from 4 bureaus to 3 (civil/livestock/security). [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1939** — Nomonhan / Khalkhin Gol battle; heavy losses; marks the end of Japanese designs on Outer Mongolia. [Narangoa 2004, p.53]
- **1939, Sept 1** — the Mongol League Autonomous Government merges with the South Chahar (察南) and North Shanxi (晋北) governments into the **Mongol United Autonomous Government (蒙疆聯合自治政府 / Mengjiang)**, capital Kalgan (Zhangjiakou); Mongol-majority character dilutes. [Dudin 2015, pp.132–133 (garbled as "Mengjiang, Chanani, Zigbee"); Narangoa 2004, p.64 (as "Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government"); Hirokawa 2024] {same event, three names — reconcile}

## Era 5 — Mengjiang consolidation: the legal-economic regime (1938–1945)

All items [Dudin 2015, pp.133–135; re-verify against Sun Cunye]. Strategy framed as "war feeds war"; late-war slogan "foodstuffs are ammunition, wool is gunpowder, people are a military force." {T3 source — treat the sequence as provisional}

- **1938, Apr** — Law on general economic mobilization.
- **1938, Oct 25** — Law restricting wool export + implementing decree (Joint Council).
- **1939, Jun 10** — United Council Law No.22 restricting cattle export.
- **1939, Oct 1** — Decree No.11 on control of public pastures (State Administrative Yuan).
- **1939, Oct 10** — Law No.3 on control of trade; Law No.4 restricting livestock export.
- **1939, Oct 20** — Law No.8 on control of goods.
- **1939, Nov 20** — Law on price control + repurchase-price regulations.
- **1940, Jan 22 & May 6** — livestock-export law extended to asses/mules/pigs.
- **1940, Jun 14** — Ministry of Civil Affairs Decision No.10, public granaries (food requisition).
- **1940, Aug 26** — amendments to the trade-control law (decrees → Ministry of Finance directives).
- **1940, Sep 17** — penalties sharply increased (up to 3 years' labor, ¥5,000).
- **1940, Dec 1** — Law on control of food (grain).
- **1940, May (early)** — Ulaanchab League 1st Banner-League Administrative Conference (3 days): banner princes press to keep Qing-era jasag rights and recover county/reclamation land revenue; regime concedes the jasag system and the new Siregetü (lama) banner but shelves the reclaimed-land question. [Hirokawa 2024, from the 大会議事録] {the West-Inner-Mongolia local texture Boyle omits}
- **1939–1940** — De Wang in secret contact with Chiang Kai-shek (via Tai Li); exposed to the Japanese side in spring 1940. [Boyle 1972, p.133, via Hangin p.39, SINGLE-SOURCE; Hirokawa 2024] {reconcile with the repo's existing Gao Rong / Ma Hansan channel — likely distinct episodes}
- **1942, Jun 2** — Mööminggan-banner jasag Chimedrinchinhorloo, suspected of MPR contact, dies (gunshot; widely read as Japanese assassination) after detention by the Bailingmiao special agency (Asaka Shirō). [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1943** — Darhan-banner jasag Tsesdbaljur's troops raid the Bailingmiao agency; the *Köke tug* (Blue Flag) Mongolian propaganda journal appears. [Hirokawa 2024; Narangoa 2004, p.58]
- **1944, Jun** — Rinchensengge (Ulad Middle-banner jasag) becomes Ulaanchab league head, then is abducted along with Baabudorj by Fu Zuoyi and held to the war's end. [Hirokawa 2024]
- **1945, Aug** — Japan's defeat; Mengjiang dissolved. [Dudin 2015, p.136; Narangoa 2004]

## Era 6 — Collapse, CCP takeover, and land reform (1945–1966)

All items [Pount, pp.48–62; sourced to Atwood, Liu Xiaoyuan, Bulag, Sneath, Hao Fan].

- **1946** — Soviet and MPR troops withdraw; the IMPRP resumes the ex-Manchukuo Khinggan provincial institutions and holds the strongest de-facto power in East Mongolia.
- **1947, May** — the IMPRP is formally absorbed into the CCP at Chengde (credited to Ulanhu); the Inner Mongolian Autonomous Region is founded May 1.
- **1947, Nov** — first wave of land reform in IMPRP-controlled areas under "General Principles of Land Tenure of China"; upheaval concentrated in Khinggan, Naun Mören, Hölön Buir, Jirim, Juu-Uda, Josotu (where ~1.13M of ~1.5M Mongols lived). Excess violence tied to the hunt for "Japanese collaborators." {Khafungga's role in the 1947–48 reform is contested: Liu credits Khafungga with driving radical class struggle; Wang Shusheng's Ulanhu biography credits Ulanhu — record both}
- **1948, Jul** — Harbin conference for IMAR CCP cadres; Ulanhu credited with getting the "mistakes" recognized and a more conservative IMAR line adopted.
- **1951** — Tümed "peaceful land reform": Mongol peasants given plots twice the size of Han peasants'.
- **1952** — reclamation drive halted under Ulanhu's "Protect the pastures, forbid reclamation" (保护牧场，禁止开荒); the 1947–52 "reclamation fever" had turned ~12M mu of pasture to farmland.
- **1953** — First Five-Year Plan begins; Mutual Aid Teams.
- **1955** — Producers' Cooperatives; Jul 1955 Mao's "cooperativizing agriculture" mission; 22,030 peasants from Shandong/Hebei/Shanxi brought to cultivate IMAR.
- **1958, Aug 13–17** — Baidaihe convention; People's Communes; by Feb 1959, ~81,000 pastoral Mongol households in 152 pastoral communes holding 90% of livestock.
- **1963** — Ulanhu publishes/circulates Mao's 1935 promises to minorities — later a major charge in his **1966** purge.

## Big source-conflicts recorded (not resolved)

**A. The 1936 Mongol Military Government cluster.** April 1936 (Hirokawa) vs the 12 May 1936 founding at Dehua (Chinese local histories) vs the 20 May 1936 Manchukuo "root-policy toward Mongols" act (Narangoa p.64). These are probably three different things — a formation, a ceremony, and a Manchukuo policy directive — not one event dated three ways. Keep them as separate lines until a source reconciles them.

**B. The Mengjiang government-body genealogy (the tangle an earlier repo pass flagged).** In dated order, these are distinct bodies, not synonyms:
1. 1934 Apr — Mongol Local Autonomy Political Council (蒙政会, Bailingmiao)
2. 1936 Apr/May — Mongol Military Government (蒙古軍政府, Dehua)
3. 1937 Sep–Oct — South Chahar + North Shansi governments; Federated Autonomous Government of Mongolia
4. 1937 Nov 22 — United Committee of Mengchiang (蒙疆聯合委員会 / Mökyö Rengö Iinkai) ← Boyle's "United Committee"
5. 1937 Dec — Mongol League Autonomous Government (蒙古聯盟自治政府, Hohhot)
6. 1939 Sep 1 — Mongol United Autonomous Government (蒙疆聯合自治政府, Kalgan)

Map the repo's "Joint Committee of Mongolian Territories" / "Mengjiang Federation Committee" onto #4 and/or #6 deliberately; don't let an agent auto-merge.

**Repo note added when this chronology was ingested:** items #3 and #5 above share the same Chinese lineage name (蒙古聯盟自治政府) and, per this chronology's own Era 4 log, appear to be the same body at two stages — the Oct 27–29 princes' convocation that formed it, and its Dec 1937 formal setup/occupation of Baotou — rather than two distinct bodies. This genealogy list numbers them separately regardless. This is recorded as an apparent internal tension between this chronology's own two representations of the body (era-log vs. genealogy list), not resolved by the atlas; see the [Mongolian Allied League Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-league-autonomous-government) entry.

**C. Gada Meiren, memory vs fact.** The song and the 1979 narrative poem (composed ~1950s) make Zhang Zuolin the villain, though Zhang was assassinated in 1928, before the Darhan land sale; the actual warlord backer was Zhang Xueliang's Fengtien regime. Some low-tier sources also wrongly say the settlers were Japanese. Record the folk-memory version and the event facts side by side.

**D. Gungsangnorbu dates.** Birth 1871 vs 1872 (Ka La Qin Palace: born in the palace 1872); death 1930 vs 1931. Leave unsettled pending Jagchid 1979.
