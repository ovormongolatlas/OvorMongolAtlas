Wu Heling was the Josotu League's delegate to a Mongol Delegation formed in Beiping in the late 1920s during the Kuomintang's Northern Expedition period; Jagchid, then a junior high school student whose father was politically active in the same circles, describes the delegation choosing Wu as its spokesman after Prince Sodnamrabdan of Shilingol League threw his support behind him (Jagchid). The years given here bracket the period of his activity documented in the sources used for this entry, not birth and death dates, which are not established by those sources.

## Mediator and rival to Demchugdongrub

Through the 1930s, Wu Heling headed the Nanjing Office of the Mongolian League and Banner Delegates and sat on the Mongolian-Tibetan Affairs Commission, positioning himself as a habitual mediator between Mongol leaders and the Chinese central government (Jagchid). This mediating role repeatedly put him at odds with [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub): Jagchid records recurring political conflict between the two men even as they were sometimes forced into cooperation by circumstance, and Demchugdongrub at one point argued directly against what Jagchid calls "Wu Heling's great accomplishment," the Organizational Law of Mongolian Leagues, Tribes, and Banners (Jagchid). Some of Demchugdongrub's own circle suspected Wu of being too close to the Chinese government to be fully trusted — a suspicion Jagchid neither fully confirms nor dismisses.

## Office under Mengjiang, and exile to Japan

Wu Heling held a long sequence of senior offices across Mengjiang's successive governments, including — per a modern academic summary of his career — deputy general secretary of the Mongolian Military Government (1936–1937), chairman of the senate (1937–1939 and 1940–1941), and chairman of the State Administrative Council (1941–1944), among other posts (Dudin). Jagchid, who served for a time as Wu's personal secretary, gives a more intimate account of one period in this career: Wu's effective exile to Japan, arranged by the Japanese advisor Kanai Shoji to remove him from Mengjiang politics under the pretext of deepening his knowledge of Japan. Jagchid accompanied him there from 1938, and the two studied together at Waseda University, though Jagchid found their working relationship difficult and resigned his secretarial post after about a year and a half (Jagchid).

## The 1949 Alashan government

After Mengjiang's collapse, Wu Heling remained active in Mongol politics. In 1949 he joined [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub)'s final effort at self-rule, the [Mongolian Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-government-alashan) based at Dingyuanying in Alashan, where he was elected speaker of the 31-member Mongolian Congress at the August 1949 delegate conference — a detail corroborated independently by both Jagchid's firsthand account and a modern academic history of the episode (Jagchid; Dudin).
