"Mengjiang" is not the formal name of any single government; it is the name that came into common use — in Chinese, Japanese (as Mōkyō), and subsequently in English-language scholarship — for the Japanese-sponsored Mongol autonomous territory administered from [Kalgan](/entry/kalgan) and [Hohhot](/entry/hohhot) between the mid-1930s and 1945. Jagchid dates the name's emergence specifically to the formation of the Japanese-run "Joint Committee of Mongolian Territories" on November 22, 1937, which unified [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub)'s Mongol government with separate Japanese puppet regimes in Southern Chahar and Northern Shanxi (Jagchid). In looser usage, "Mengjiang" is sometimes applied back to the whole run of Demchugdongrub-led governments starting with the [Mongolian Military Government](/entry/mongolian-military-government) of 1936, since it was the first of these governments to be explicitly Japanese-sponsored — hence the startYear given here is approximate.

Across its existence the territory was governed by a succession of differently named administrations, each with its own entry in this atlas:

- [Mongolian Military Government](/entry/mongolian-military-government) (1936–1937)
- [Mongolian Allied League Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-league-autonomous-government) (1937–1939)
- [Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-autonomous-government) (1939–1941)
- [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state) (1941–1945), the final and internationally best-known iteration

Demchugdongrub led all four in some capacity, though not always as full chairman from the outset — see his entry and each government's own entry for the specifics of when he held nominal versus real authority. The territory dissolved with Japan's defeat in August 1945.

## A 1943 propaganda account

A wartime account by Alfred Lückenhaus describes Mengjiang from within its final years — but it is not neutral reportage. Lückenhaus wrote for the DNB, Nazi Germany's official state news agency, and published this piece in *The XXth Century*, a German-run, pro-Axis magazine operating in Japanese-occupied Shanghai. It is propaganda about a Japanese client state, produced by an ally of Japan's own Axis partner, and its economic, military, and "pacification" claims should be treated as wartime boosterism — likely inflated or unverifiable — rather than as established fact. Every claim drawn from it below is attributed explicitly rather than stated as plain fact.

**Self-image.** According to Lückenhaus's 1943 account, the regime wished to present itself as unified, developing, and orderly — a "remarkably quiet and pacified territory" building schools, an army, and industry, reforming Lamaist religious institutions, and doing all of this under Japanese "guidance" (Lückenhaus). Its rhetorical alignment with Axis wartime messaging is explicit at points: the account claims the people of Mengjiang already enjoyed "freedom from want and freedom from fear" under Japanese tutelage — an unmistakable jab at Franklin Roosevelt's wartime rhetoric (Lückenhaus). [Wu Heling](/entry/wu-heling), described in the piece as Mengjiang's "Prime Minister," is quoted framing the regime's purpose as twofold: realizing Mongol unification and helping Japan win the war, presented as complementary rather than competing aims (Lückenhaus). This is the regime's own wartime messaging, relayed by a sympathetic foreign correspondent — not this atlas's assessment of actual conditions. Its specific economic claims follow the same pattern and should be read the same way: the account cites, for instance, an estimate of 29.3 billion tons of coal reserves in the Tatung field alone, a figure with no independent verification available here (Lückenhaus).

**Administrative and demographic detail.** Some specifics in the account corroborate or add texture to institutional detail already established elsewhere in this atlas, and are usable with attribution. Lückenhaus describes Mengjiang as having begun in 1937 as a federation of three separate governments — the United Mongolian Leagues (capital Houho, which the account glosses as "formerly Suiyuan" and which is almost certainly [Hohhot](/entry/hohhot)), North Shansi (capital Datong), and South Chahar (capital [Kalgan](/entry/kalgan)) (Lückenhaus). At the start of 1943 the North Shansi and South Chahar governments were abolished and converted into ordinary provinces, and Lückenhaus describes Kalgan as thereafter "regarded as the capital" administratively — though, by his own account, League affairs continued to be directed mostly from Houho, which Mongol leaders reportedly hoped would eventually become Mengjiang's real capital, a nuance worth keeping rather than flattening into a single clean "capital" claim (Lückenhaus). He describes the army as under the supreme command of Li Shou-hsin (Li Shouxin) and the regime's calendar as dated from Chinggis Khan, both consistent with detail given elsewhere in this atlas (Lückenhaus; Jagchid).

The account's demographic figures, which Lückenhaus attributes to Japanese sources, are worth recording as a striking claim in their own right: of a total population of roughly 6 million people in Mengjiang, fewer than about 240,000 were Mongol, with the remainder overwhelmingly Han Chinese alongside "several hundred thousand" Chinese Muslims (Lückenhaus). The same official figures he cites also claimed 1.3 million Mongols living in Manchukuo and another 900,000 in Soviet-aligned Outer Mongolia. If accurate, a nominally Mongol state governed a population that was, by a wide margin, not Mongol at all — though as with all figures from this source, the exact numbers should be read as an Axis-aligned wartime account's presentation of Japanese-supplied statistics, not as independently verified fact.

**The flag.** Mengjiang's flag visually encoded this same ethnic hierarchy. Jagchid describes it as seven horizontal stripes: a broad red band in the center for the Japanese, two narrow white stripes for Muslims, two slightly wider blue stripes for Mongols, and yellow bands top and bottom, equal in width to the blue, for "the Chinese and other nationalities" — the whole design meant to symbolize Japan as the country toward which the other nationalities of East Asia should "incline," unifying under the new regime (Jagchid, p. 219). Jagchid also records that the flag was imposed over Demchugdongrub's own objections: he initially refused it, preferring the earlier Mongolian Military Government's flag, and only relented under direct pressure from Japanese officials (Jagchid).

## A critical counterweight: modern scholarship

Where Lückenhaus's account is the regime's own wartime self-presentation, modern scholarship offers a very different picture. Tuo Jianing's 2019 study of Hui Muslim intellectuals under Mengjiang — part of a broader look at the [autonomy movement's](/entry/mongolian-political-affairs-council) legacy — draws on Japanese-era records and Chinese-language local histories to give a more precise founding chronology and a demographic picture that undercuts the regime's own Mongol-nationalist framing (Tuo).

**Founding chronology.** Tuo dates the regime's formation in more granular steps than other sources in this atlas, though exact dates vary slightly across accounts. In 1936, as the calendar was reset to the 731st year of Chinggis Khan, [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub) established the Mongolian army headquarters in the Sonid (Sünit) Right Banner — the same banner as [West Sünit](/entry/west-sunit-ruling-house), "Right" being equivalent to "West" — and a Mongolian military government at Dehua (Tuo). Following the Marco Polo Bridge Incident of July 7, 1937, Demchugdongrub founded the Mongolia Federation Autonomous Government in [Hohhot](/entry/hohhot) (formerly Guisui, the prewar capital of Suiyuan Province) on October 27, 1937, with Japanese backing (Tuo). On November 22, 1937, a Mengjiang Federation Committee formed at Kalgan, uniting three puppet governments — South Chahar (at Kalgan), North Jin/Shanxi (at Datong), and the Mongolia Federation government (at Hohhot) — which finally merged into a single Mengjiang Government on September 1, 1939 (Tuo).

**Demographics: a state overwhelmingly not Mongol.** Tuo cites Japanese wartime records giving Mengjiang's population in 1939 as roughly 5.21 million Han Chinese, 290,000 Mongols, and 94,000 Hui Muslims; a separate 1941 Japanese survey put the population at about 4.86 million Han and roughly 300,000 Mongols, with the recorded Hui population dropping sharply to about 37,000 (Tuo). By either count, Mongols made up under six percent of the population of a state whose entire claimed purpose was Mongol self-rule — the central irony of Mengjiang as a political project, here confirmed by scholarship rather than by contemporary propaganda.

**A multi-ethnic reality.** Mengjiang's Hui Muslim minority, though far smaller than the Han or Mongol populations, had its own Japanese-sponsored institutions — an Islamic Association of the Northwest, its own schools, its own press — through which Japanese authorities sought to instrumentalize Hui identity for colonial purposes much as they did Mongol identity (Tuo). Not every minority group accepted the regime's nationalist framing on its own terms: Tuo notes that while the Mengjiang government dated its calendar from Chinggis Khan, the Baotou Great Mosque continued using the Republic of China calendar, marking woven carpets with "the 30th year of the Republic of China" in 1941 rather than the regime's own year 736 (Tuo). This entry does not attempt a full account of the Hui under Mengjiang or of Wu Yaocheng, the Hui educator at the center of Tuo's study — that would be a natural future expansion of this atlas.
