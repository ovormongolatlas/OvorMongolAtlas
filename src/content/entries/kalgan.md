Kalgan (present-day Zhangjiakou) anchored one of the two main caravan corridors linking China proper to Mongolia and Russia. Charleux, drawing on Western travelers' accounts, describes a well-frequented trade route — carrying tea in particular — that ran from Beijing through Chahar to Kalgan and on to Urga, Kyakhta, and Siberia; historical travelers who used it include the Scottish physician and diplomat John Bell and Lorenz Lange, on a Russian embassy from Saint Petersburg to Beijing in 1719–1722, Egor Timkowski's 1820–21 Russian embassy, and Catherine de Bourboulon, the first European woman to cross Mongolia, in 1862 (Charleux). This was a separate route from the Hohhot-centered caravan traffic described in that entry — many travelers used one or the other, not both (Charleux).

Kalgan's greater significance to this atlas, though, is later and political: it became the administrative capital of [Demchugdongrub](/entry/demchugdongrub)'s successive [Mengjiang](/entry/mengjiang) governments and, for a period, the single most important Mongol-affiliated city in the region — outranking [Hohhot](/entry/hohhot) even though Hohhot was itself a Mengjiang seat of government at various points.

## Mengjiang's administrative capital

Kalgan was also the seat of the [South Chahar Autonomous Government](/entry/south-chahar-autonomous-government), the Chinese puppet regime established there September 4, 1937, weeks before Demchugdongrub's own government took Hohhot (Boyle; Hirokawa 2024, via this atlas's master chronology). The [Mongolian Allied Autonomous Government](/entry/mongolian-allied-autonomous-government), founded at Kalgan on September 1, 1939, named the city its capital outright in its own founding charter — "the location of the capital is Kalgan," in Jagchid's rendering of Article 5 of that government's Outline of Organization — and Kalgan remained the seat of Mengjiang's central institutions through the [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state) that succeeded it in 1941 (Jagchid).

Jagchid describes the practical effect in blunt terms: after the Japanese-run [Joint Committee of Mongolian Territories](/entry/united-committee-of-mengchiang) was established there in November 1937, the regime opened the Mengjiang Bank, the Mengjiang Railway Bureau, the Longyan Iron Mine, the Datong Coal Mine, the Mengjiang Daily News, and the Mongolian Wool and Hide Export Cooperative, all centered in Kalgan; politically and economically, he writes, Kalgan became the regional center, "overshadowing Hohhot" — which he says was soon regarded as the unofficial headquarters of a lesser regime (Jagchid). Jagchid frames this concentration of power in Kalgan as a defeat for Demchugdongrub specifically: it consolidated the authority of the Japanese supreme advisor [Kanai Shoji](/entry/kanai-shoji) at the prince's expense, part of what Jagchid casts as a losing political battle between the two men (Jagchid).

## A Mongol print-culture hub

Kalgan was also where Mengjiang's main Mongol-language literary press operated. As this atlas's [Saichungga](/entry/saichungga) entry records (following Atwood), the poet's work appeared chiefly in *Manduqu Mongγol* ("Mongolia Rising"), the [Mongolian Autonomous State](/entry/mongolian-autonomous-state)'s principal journal of opinion and literature, published from Kalgan and edited by [Gombojab Hangin](/entry/gombojab-hangin) — a detail this entry relays from that source rather than re-deriving from Atwood's article directly.

A second Mengjiang-era Mongol periodical is recorded here with more caution: a journal this atlas renders *Temür kerem* ("Iron Wall"), whose inaugural-issue cover survives as an image from a Chinese-language source. That romanization is read off an OCR'd Chinese caption rather than confirmed against a second, independent source, and is flagged accordingly — it should be treated as provisional until verified. If accurate, it places a second Mongol-language periodical in the same Kalgan print milieu as *Manduqu Mongγol*, part of the same wartime Mongol print culture the Mongolian Autonomous State fostered in the city.

## Regional context: the Chahar frontier (Lattimore)

Kalgan sat at the edge of Chahar province, a region the geographer Owen Lattimore studied firsthand in the 1930s and described, in a 1938 interpretive essay, as caught in a slow-motion ecological and economic transformation — his framing, offered here as regional background rather than a claim about Kalgan itself. Lattimore observed that Chahar and neighboring Ulanchab League Mongols were being squeezed into progressively narrower grazing grounds as Chinese colonists took over the best winter pastures for farming, degrading the remaining Mongol pastoral economy even as some of that same colonized land — its soil exhausted by agriculture — was increasingly turned over to Chinese-run grazing instead (Lattimore, "Geographical Factor"). He framed this as part of a longer historical pattern along the Mongol-Chinese frontier in which, over time, some Chinese settlers were assimilated into pastoral, nomadic life while some Mongols were absorbed into Chinese agricultural society (Lattimore, "Geographical Factor").
